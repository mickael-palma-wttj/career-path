- **Real examples** by level

## üìä Understanding Rubric Structure

Each rubric file follows this structure:

### 1. **Definition**
What the criterion means and why it matters.

### 2. **Level-by-Level Breakdown**
How the criterion manifests at each level (Junior through Principal):
- **0.0-1.0**: Developing - Learning this skill
- **1.0-2.0**: Competent - Reliable execution with support
- **2.0-3.0**: Proficient - Consistent, independent performance
- **3.0-3.5**: Expert - Deep expertise, mentors others
- **3.5-4.0**: Authority - Organization-wide impact

### 3. **How It Manifests in Work**
Concrete examples of what you'd observe at each level.

### 4. **Assessment Questions**
Questions to ask or observations to make when evaluating.

### 5. **Evidence to Look For**
Specific indicators that someone is performing at each level.

### 6. **Common Misconceptions**
Myths or misunderstandings about what this criterion means.

### 7. **Related Skills**
Other criteria that support or complement this one.

### 8. **Coaching Strategies**
How to help someone develop in this area.

### 9. **Real Examples**
Scenario-based examples showing different performance levels.

## üéØ How to Use These Rubrics

### For Assessment
1. **Read the criterion definition** to understand what you're evaluating
2. **Review the level descriptions** and find where your IC fits best
3. **Look for evidence** in the observation section
4. **Ask assessment questions** to yourself or in conversation
5. **Rate** based on the level-by-level breakdown

### For Development Planning
1. **Identify current level** for the criterion
2. **Review what "proficient" (2.0-3.0) looks like** at your IC's current level
3. **Use coaching strategies** to build skills
4. **Provide stretch opportunities** that develop this criterion
5. **Monitor progress** through 1:1s and feedback

### For Calibration
1. **Use real examples** to align understanding with other managers
2. **Compare your assessment** across different ICs
3. **Discuss edge cases** using the rubric as reference
4. **Refine your understanding** of each criterion

### For Conversations
1. **Share the rubric** with IC to show what "good" looks like
2. **Use concrete examples** from the rubric
3. **Set development goals** based on criterion levels
4. **Track progress** using the level descriptions

## üîÑ Key Principles

### Contextual Judgment
- Ratings are **contextual** to the level
- A "2.0" in writing code as a Senior is different from "2.0" as a Junior
- Use the level-specific descriptions to calibrate

### Evidence-Based
- Rate based on **observed behaviors**, not intuition
- Look for multiple data points (projects, feedback, behavior)
- Document specific examples

### Developmental Orientation
- Rubrics are for **growth**, not just evaluation
- Focus on "what's next" not just "where you are"
- Use for coaching and development planning

### Holisticism
- Evaluate the **whole person**, not individual moments
- Average out good and bad days
- Consider patterns, not exceptions

## üéì Tips for Managers

### Getting Started
1. **Pick 1-2 criteria** you struggle assessing
2. **Read those rubrics deeply**
3. **Apply them to real ICs** on your team
4. **Adjust your mental model** based on what you find
5. **Gradually use more criteria**

### Avoiding Pitfalls
- ‚ùå Don't rate based on a single project or day
- ‚ùå Don't confuse "different" with "better/worse"
- ‚ùå Don't rate only based on what you see directly
- ‚ùå Don't skip the evidence collection step
- ‚úÖ Do collect feedback from multiple sources
- ‚úÖ Do look at patterns over time
- ‚úÖ Do consider context and constraints
- ‚úÖ Do discuss with peers for calibration

### Developing Your Rubrics
- **Customize** the language to match your org
- **Add examples** specific to your products/services
- **Refine levels** based on actual performance in your org
- **Update regularly** as your org grows and changes
