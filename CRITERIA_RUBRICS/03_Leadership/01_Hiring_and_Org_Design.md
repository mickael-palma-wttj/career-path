# Hiring & Org Design

**Criterion:** Ability to evaluate talent, contribute to org structure, and build effective teams that compound in capability over time.

This evaluates both immediate hiring effectiveness (finding and attracting quality talent) and strategic thinking about how to organize teams and structure roles for long-term organizational health and capability.

## üéØ Definition

**Hiring & Org Design** encompasses five interconnected capabilities:

### 1. **Talent Evaluation**
The ability to accurately assess candidate quality, fit, potential, and long-term capability through structured interviews, reference checks, and work samples. Includes identifying both immediate capability and growth potential.

### 2. **Team Building**
Composing teams with the right mix of skills, experiences, perspectives, and personalities. Understanding how to balance senior mentors with junior growth, specialists with generalists, leaders with strong individual contributors.

### 3. **Organizational Structure**
Thinking strategically about how to organize work, roles, and responsibilities to maximize effectiveness. Understanding trade-offs between specialization and flexibility, hierarchy and autonomy, centralization and distribution.

### 4. **Diversity & Inclusion**
Actively building teams and hiring pools that are diverse across dimension that matter (background, experience, perspective, identity). Recognizing and mitigating bias in hiring.

### 5. **Long-term Capability**
Building for the org you want to be, not just filling immediate gaps. Developing talent internally while bringing in fresh perspectives. Creating career paths that retain high performers.

## üìä Detailed Level Descriptions

### üü¶ Developing (0.0 - 1.0)

**Core Behaviors:**
- Participates in hiring interviews but relies heavily on others' judgments
- Struggles to articulate evaluation criteria; decisions based on impressions
- Doesn't think about organizational structure or team composition
- May have hiring bias they're unaware of
- Focuses narrowly on filling role with "someone qualified"
- No demonstrated track record of successful hires

**What You'd See:**
- "They seem smart" or "I liked them" as primary feedback rather than specific technical or cultural assessment
- Surprised when hire doesn't work out
- Doesn't ask structured interview questions; conversation is unfocused
- Unaware of organizational gaps or inefficiencies
- Hires don't integrate well into teams

**What Success Looks Like:**
- Participates actively in interviews (not passive)
- Can articulate basic criteria for a role
- Begins to notice patterns in what makes good hires
- Opens up to feedback on hiring process

**Development Focus:**
- Learn to interview with structured approach
- Build vocabulary for evaluating talent
- Exposure to org structure thinking through observation

---

### üü© Competent (1.0 - 2.0)

**Core Behaviors:**
- Can interview effectively and contribute meaningful hiring feedback
- Understands basic hiring criteria; evaluates against job requirements
- Makes good hiring decisions most of the time
- Begins to think about team composition and balance
- Aware of organizational structure but limited strategic thinking
- Has had several successful hires; demonstrates improving judgment

**What You'd See:**
- Clear feedback in interviews: "Strong technical skills in X. Struggles with Y. Would pair well with Z."
- Considers team needs when evaluating candidates ("We need someone who can mentor juniors")
- Can identify an org problem: "Our backend team is all senior; we need mid-level developers"
- Takes initiative to improve hiring process
- Hires tend to succeed and integrate well
- Begins to advocate for better hiring practices

**Specific Examples:**
- "When I interviewed Sarah, I noticed she asked great questions about team dynamics and mentoring. That mattered because our team needs someone who'll help develop junior engineers."
- "We should change our interview format because candidates feel too rushed to show their real thinking."
- "This role needs someone comfortable with ambiguity and learning quickly, not just someone who knows our tech stack."

**What Success Looks Like:**
- Own hiring for at least one role from sourcing through final decision
- Can mentor a junior person on interview technique
- Makes hiring recommendations that are sound and well-reasoned
- Notices and speaks up about team composition gaps

**Development Focus:**
- Own complete hiring cycles (don't just participate)
- Learn to think strategically about team composition
- Study org structure and how it affects effectiveness

### üüß Proficient (2.0 - 3.0)

**Core Behaviors:**
- Consistently strong hiring judgment; candidates they champion tend to be great additions
- Thinks strategically about team composition and balance
- Can articulate thoughtful org structure principles and trade-offs
- Proactively identifies structural issues and proposes solutions
- Actively works to build diverse teams; recognizes and addresses bias
- Track record of building effective teams that stay together and produce results
- Mentors others on hiring best practices

**What You'd See:**
- "I want to hire a mid-level IC and a junior to create a development pipeline. Currently we're top-heavy."
- "The way we've structured our teams means critical knowledge lives in one person. We should redistribute these responsibilities."
- "When hiring for this role, I'm looking for someone who adds perspective we're missing, not just technical fit."
- Successfully predicts which candidates will be high performers
- Proactively improves hiring process: "Let's change our interview rubric to better assess X"
- Builds teams with strong dynamics and low attrition
- Can articulate why org structure isn't optimal and concrete alternatives

**Specific Examples:**
- "I realized our team is all architects. We need hands-on developers who will challenge us and do the implementation. That changes what we look for."
- "Given the growth we're planning, I'd recommend splitting the team here and creating a new lead role to distribute authority and develop someone for promotion."
- "Our hiring questions weren't testing for collaboration. I redesigned the interview to better assess that."

**Evidence:**
- Own hiring for multiple roles; consistent success
- Team composition is intentional and well-balanced
- Can articulate team needs and hire against them strategically
- Mentors others on hiring (training new interviewers, reviewing interview feedback)
- Identified org structural issues and improvements
- Diverse teams; can discuss active work on inclusion

**What Success Looks Like:**
- Lead hiring for a team (multiple roles, own the strategy)
- Design interview process improvements adopted by others
- Build team that's diverse, capable, and retains well
- Make org structure recommendations that leadership considers seriously

**Development Focus:**
- Think at org level, not just team level
- Learn to navigate politics of organizational change
- Develop executive presence to influence org-wide decisions

## ‚ùì Assessment Questions

### For Any Level:
- **Tell me about your best hire.** What did you see in them? How did you assess them? Why do you think they worked out?
- **Tell me about a hire that didn't work out.** What happened? What would you do differently?
- **What criteria do you use to evaluate someone for [specific role]?** (Probe: Is it well-articulated? Based on requirements? Thoughtful about team fit?)

### For Competent+ Levels:
- **How do you think about team composition?** What makes a good team? How do you balance experience, skill, personality?
- **Describe a time you identified an org structure problem.** What was the problem? How did you think about solving it?
- **How do you work to build diverse teams?** What does that mean to you? What have you actually done?

### For Proficient+ Levels:
- **You're growing from 5 to 10 people on your team. How would you think about who to hire and why?** (Probe: Strategic thinking? Understanding capability needs?)
- **Walk me through a hiring decision where you had to choose between technical fit and other factors.** How did you think about that trade-off?
- **What would you change about how our org is structured?** Why? What would be better about it?
- **Tell me about someone you've successfully mentored on hiring.**

### For Expert+ Levels:
- **What's your philosophy on hiring and team building?** How does it connect to org success?
- **You're an advisor on our overall talent strategy. What would you recommend we focus on?**
- **Tell me about building multiple strong teams. What patterns did you notice across them?**
- **How do you think about the relationship between org structure and people strategy?**

## üìö Detailed Development Guidance

### Improving Hiring Judgment

**Pattern 1: Too Impressed by Raw Intelligence**
- **Problem:** Hires people who are smart but not collaborative, can't grow with org, difficult to work with
- **Fix:** Add interview questions on collaboration: "Tell me about a time you needed help. How did you get it?" "Describe a time you disagreed with a teammate. How did you handle it?"
- **Watch For:** References that talk about independence but not teamwork

**Pattern 2: Hiring for Today, Not Tomorrow**
- **Problem:** Hire people who fit immediate need but org grows past them quickly
- **Fix:** In each hiring cycle, ask: "What will we need in 2 years?" Hire for growth path, not just today
- **Watch For:** Candidates who are curious learners, not just current experts

**Pattern 3: Homogenous Teams**
- **Problem:** Hire people similar to yourself; team lacks diverse perspectives
- **Fix:** Explicit diversity goals; diverse interview panels; expanded recruiting; examine your bias
- **Watch For:** Candidates from different backgrounds; expand your definition of "good"

### Improving Team Building

**Pattern 1: Wrong Mix**
- **Problem:** All senior engineers; or all junior; never enough balance
- **Fix:** Audit team: experience levels, skills, growth areas. Deliberately hire for balance
- **Watch For:** Intentional "mentors" and "learners"; different skill specialization

**Pattern 2: No Pipeline**
- **Problem:** Can't promote because no one is ready; team stagnates
- **Fix:** Build career ladder; create stretching projects; develop junior people intentionally
- **Watch For:** Succession plan; people progressing

**Pattern 3: Dysfunction**
- **Problem:** Team has personality conflicts; people leaving; culture bad
- **Fix:** Examine hiring decisions; did you ignore signals? Add soft skills assessment; be more rigorous on collaboration
- **Watch For:** Why did people leave? What could have been predicted?

### Improving Org Design Thinking

**Key Principle:** Structure follows strategy, not the reverse. Ask:
- What do we need to accomplish?
- What decisions need to be made?
- Who should make them?
- How do we avoid bottlenecks?
- How do we maintain culture as we scale?

**Common Org Mistakes:**
- Organizing by titles instead of work
- Making one person gatekeeper for critical decisions
- No clear authority/accountability
- Structure that doesn't match how work actually flows
- Org doesn't scale with company growth

**How to Think About It:**
- Draw your org on whiteboard
- For each team/role, ask: Is this decision-making authority clear? Is anyone bottlenecked? Is critical knowledge in one person?
- What would change if you doubled in size?

## üí° Key Principles

### 1. **Hiring is Your Multiplier**
You're only as good as the people around you. Great hiring creates a compounding advantage over time. Bad hiring creates drag you can't overcome.

### 2. **Culture is Built Through Hiring**
Every hire either strengthens or weakens your culture. Your hiring bar *is* your culture.

### 3. **Structure Enables or Prevents Good Work**
The right org structure makes good work easy; the wrong structure makes it impossible. This is strategic.

### 4. **Diversity Requires Active Work**
Homogenous teams happen by default. Diversity requires intentional process design and bias mitigation.

### 5. **Long-term Thinking Matters**
Build for the company you're going to be, not just who you are today. This means developing talent internally while bringing in fresh perspectives.

### 6. **Data + Intuition**
Good hiring combines structured evaluation (rubric, criteria, evidence) with gut feel about potential. Purely data-driven or purely intuitive both fail.
