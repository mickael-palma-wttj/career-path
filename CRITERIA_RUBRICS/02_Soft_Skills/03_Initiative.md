# Initiative & Ownership

**Criterion:** Proactive problem-solving, self-direction, and ability to identify and act on improvements without prompting. Taking ownership of outcomes rather than waiting to be told what to do.

Initiative is the difference between people who execute tasks and people who drive results. It's about ownership, energy, and the willingness to improve things without being asked. People with high initiative create opportunities where others see only constraints.

## üéØ Definition

**Initiative & Ownership** encompasses five interconnected capabilities:

### 1. **Problem Spotting**
Ability to see issues, bottlenecks, and inefficiencies before they're pointed out. Not just noticing them, but recognizing they matter and that someone should fix them.

### 2. **Self-Direction**
Acting without being told. Making decisions about what deserves attention, prioritizing, and moving forward independently. Requires judgment about what matters.

### 3. **Ownership Mentality**
Taking responsibility for outcomes, not just tasks. If something isn't working, it's your problem to solve (within your scope). Accountability is personal.

### 4. **Follow-Through**
Starting things and finishing them. Not just identifying problems but seeing solutions through to completion or handing off appropriately.

### 5. **Risk-Taking & Learning**
Willing to try new approaches and learn from failures. Not reckless, but not overly cautious either. Comfortable with calculated risks.

## üìö Detailed Development Guidance

### Improving Problem Spotting

**How to Develop It:**
1. **Stay curious** - Why is this process designed this way?
2. **Ask questions** - What's inefficient? What's repeated? What's frustrating?
3. **Talk to users** - What's hard about this?
4. **Look for friction** - Where do people complain?
5. **Measure results** - Where are we failing to meet goals?
6. **Think about future** - What will break as we scale?

**Common Mistakes:**
- ‚ùå Only seeing problems in your domain
- ‚ùå Not distinguishing important from annoying
- ‚ùå Spotting but not speaking up
- ‚ùå Complaining without constructing solution
- ‚ùå Overestimating importance of problems

**How You Know It's Working:**
- You notice things others don't
- Problems you spot matter to org
- People ask your opinion on issues
- You bring solutions, not just problems

### Improving Self-Direction

**Framework for Deciding When to Act:**
1. **Is it clearly in my responsibility?** - Then definitely act
2. **Is it close to my responsibility?** - Probably act, maybe ask after
3. **Is it adjacent but someone else owns it?** - Ask first or mention idea
4. **Is it totally someone else's domain?** - Suggest, don't own

**Common Mistakes:**
- ‚ùå Waiting for permission for everything
- ‚ùå Acting on things that aren't your responsibility
- ‚ùå Not understanding your actual scope
- ‚ùå Being paralyzed by uncertainty
- ‚ùå Acting on things without understanding context

**How to Get Clarity:**
- Ask directly: "When should I act vs. ask?"
- Test small: Act on small thing, see response
- Understand org: Learn how decisions flow
- Observe leaders: How do they decide?

### Improving Ownership

**Ownership Means:**
- Taking responsibility for outcomes, not just tasks
- Fixing problems rather than reporting them
- Caring about long-term impact, not just immediate task
- Owning problems until solved, not just started

**Building It:**
1. **Reframe from "task" to "outcome"** - Not "ship this feature" but "solve this customer problem"
2. **Take responsibility** - "I own this" not "I'm supposed to"
3. **See it through** - Don't hand off incomplete
4. **Own the impact** - "Here's what changed" not "I built this"
5. **Learn from results** - Did it work? Why? What next?

**Red Flags (Low Ownership):**
- ‚ùå "That's not my job"
- ‚ùå "I did what I was asked"
- ‚ùå Hands off incomplete work
- ‚ùå Doesn't follow up on impact
- ‚ùå Blames others when things don't work

### Improving Follow-Through

**Good Follow-Through:**
1. **Commit clearly** - "I'm going to..."
2. **Plan steps** - What does done look like?
3. **Execute** - Actually do the work
4. **Check results** - Did it work?
5. **Learn** - What would I do differently?

**Common Mistakes:**
- ‚ùå Lots of starts, few finishes
- ‚ùå Abandons things when hard
- ‚ùå Doesn't measure results
- ‚ùå Moves to next thing without closure
- ‚ùå Forgets what you committed to

**How You Know It's Working:**
- People can count on your commitments
- Things you start get finished
- Results are visible
- People trust your word

### Taking Smart Risks

**Calculated Risk Framework:**
1. **What's the potential upside?** - Is it worth the risk?
2. **What's the potential downside?** - What could go wrong?
3. **What's reversible?** - Can we undo if wrong?
4. **What do we learn?** - Even if failed, what do we learn?
5. **Is this my risk to take?** - Do I have authority?

**Smart Risk vs. Reckless:**
- **Smart:** "I tried a new approach. It failed but we learned that..."
- **Reckless:** "I tried without checking; broke production"
- **Overly cautious:** "I waited until conditions were perfect; missed opportunity"

**How You Know It's Working:**
- Some risks pay off
- Failures result in learning
- You improve judgment over time

## üí° Key Principles

### 1. **Initiative Compounds**
Small initiatives lead to bigger ones. Early wins build confidence. People with initiative get more opportunities to take initiative.

### 2. **Ownership Creates Accountability**
When you own something, you're accountable for outcomes. That shifts how you think about problems‚Äîthey become your problem to solve.

### 3. **You Don't Need Permission**
Most problems in your scope are yours to fix. You don't need to ask permission to improve things. Clarity on scope > endless asking.

### 4. **Failure Is Part of Initiative**
You can't take initiative without some failures. The question isn't whether you'll fail, it's whether you'll learn and try again.

### 5. **Initiative Is Contagious**
When people around you take initiative, you take more. Leaders create cultures where proactivity is normalized, not exceptional.

### 6. **Risk-Taking Requires Judgment**
Reckless risk-taking fails. Smart risk-taking requires understanding potential upside, downside, and what you can recover from.
