# Peer Feedback Request Template

Templates for requesting helpful peer feedback for promotions, evaluations, or development planning.

## üìù Template 1: Promotion Packet Feedback

**Use when:** Building promotion case, need strong supporting evidence

---

**Subject:** Feedback Request for [Name]'s Promotion Packet

Hi [Peer Name],

I'm building a promotion case for [Name] from [Current Level] to [Target Level], and I'd love your input since you've worked closely with [him/her/them] on [specific project/area].

2. Specific strengths you've observed
3. Impact [he/she/they] had on the work or team
4. Any standout moments or examples

**Context:**
[Name] has been at [Current Level] for [X time] and has been demonstrating [key next-level behavior, e.g., "technical leadership across teams"]. This feedback will be included in [his/her/their] promotion packet.

**Timeline:** Please share by [specific date, ideally 1 week out]

**What good feedback looks like:**

‚úÖ **Good Example:**
"I worked with Sarah on the Notification System project where she led the technical design. She proactively coordinated across 4 teams, ran weekly syncs, and made thoughtful trade-off decisions. When we hit a critical integration issue 2 weeks before launch, she quickly designed an elegant solution and got all teams aligned in 48 hours. Her technical leadership and communication made this complex project successful. I'd work with her again in a heartbeat."

‚ùå **Less Helpful:**
"Sarah is great to work with and does good work."

**Questions?** Let me know! And thank you for taking the time to help [Name]'s career growth.

Best,

## üìù Template 2: 360 Review Feedback

**Use when:** Regular review cycle, need balanced input

---

**Subject:** 360 Feedback Request for [Name]

Hi [Peer Name],

It's performance review time, and I'm collecting 360 feedback for [Name]. You've worked with [him/her/them] on [project/area], so your perspective would be valuable.

**What I need:**
Please answer these questions (3-5 sentences each):

1. **Strengths:** What does [Name] do particularly well?
2. **Impact:** How has [Name]'s work affected you, your team, or your projects?
3. **Growth Opportunities:** What's one area [he/she/they] could develop?
4. **Collaboration:** How effective is [Name] to work with?

**Context:**
This feedback helps me:
- Give [Name] a complete picture of [his/her/their] impact
- Identify development areas
- Support [his/her/their] continued growth

**Confidentiality:**
Your feedback will be aggregated with others and shared thematically with [Name]. I won't attribute specific quotes unless you say it's okay.

**Timeline:** Please submit by [date]

[Link to feedback form if you use one, or request via email]

Thank you for helping [Name] grow!

Best,
[Your Name]

## üìù Template 3: Development-Focused Feedback

**Use when:** IC needs constructive input for growth plan

---

**Subject:** Feedback to Help [Name] Grow

Hi [Peer Name],

[Name] is working on [his/her/their] development plan and asked me to gather feedback from people [he/she/they] respects. You came to mind because of your work together on [project/area].

**What I need:**
Brief input (3-5 sentences) on:

1. **What [Name] should keep doing:** Strengths to leverage
2. **What [Name] should start/stop doing:** Specific suggestions for growth
3. **Skills to develop:** Based on what you've observed, what would help [him/her/them] grow?

**Context:**
[Name] is focused on [specific goal, e.g., "growing toward Senior level" or "improving technical communication"]. Honest, constructive feedback will help [him/her/them] create a targeted development plan.

**How it will be used:**
I'll share this feedback directly with [Name] to help [him/her/them] identify growth priorities. This is about development, not evaluation.

**Timeline:** Please send by [date]

**Example of helpful feedback:**

"Keep doing: Your code reviews are thorough and educational. The way you explain the 'why' behind suggestions helps people learn.

Start doing: More proactive communication on project status. A couple times we were surprised by delays that you'd known about earlier.

Skills to develop: Public speaking/presentations. Your technical ideas are strong, but sharing them more broadly would increase your impact."

Thanks for investing in [Name]'s growth!

Best,
[Your Name]

## üí° Tips for Requesting Peer Feedback

### For Managers

**Choose the Right People:**
- Direct collaborators (worked together on specific projects)
- Cross-functional partners (PM, design, other teams)
- People at different levels (peers, senior folks, ICs they've mentored)
- Mix of close collaborators and occasional partners
- Aim for 5-8 people for promotions, 3-5 for regular reviews

**Timing Matters:**
- Ask early (at least 1 week before you need it)
- Don't ask during busy periods (end of quarter, launches)
- Request soon after projects end while details are fresh

**Make It Easy:**
- Be specific about what you need
- Provide context on how it will be used
- Show examples of good feedback
- Give a clear deadline
- Make submission easy (form, email, whatever works)

**Follow Up:**
- Send reminder 2-3 days before deadline
- Thank people when they submit
- Share outcome if appropriate ("Thanks for your input‚ÄîSarah got promoted!")

---

### For ICs (Self-Collecting)

**Ask Strategically:**
- Focus on recent collaborations
- Include people whose opinion you value
- Mix of people who've seen different aspects of your work
- Don't only ask friends‚Äîseek diverse perspectives

**Be Genuine:**
- Express authentic desire to grow
- Make it clear you want honesty, not flattery
- Show how you'll use the feedback
- Thank people sincerely

**Time It Right:**
- After successful projects
- Before review cycles or promotion discussions
- When planning development goals
- After reaching milestones

## ‚úÖ Quality Check: Good vs. Poor Feedback

### ‚≠ê High-Quality Feedback

**Characteristics:**
- Specific examples and situations
- Describes behaviors and impact
- Balanced (strengths and growth areas)
- Actionable (person can do something with it)
- Evidence-based (observable facts, not assumptions)

**Example:**
"Maria led the API redesign project where she coordinated across 3 teams. She ran effective technical syncs, documented decisions clearly, and made thoughtful trade-offs between performance and maintainability. When Backend team had concerns, she scheduled a deep-dive session and found a solution everyone supported. One area for growth: earlier escalation when timelines slip‚Äîwe learned about a 2-week delay only 1 week before the original deadline. Overall, excellent technical leadership and collaboration."

---

### ‚ùå Poor-Quality Feedback

**Characteristics:**
- Generic and vague
- No specific examples
- Only positive or only negative
- Not actionable
- Based on assumptions, not observations

**Example:**
"Maria is a good engineer and nice to work with. She does quality work."

## üéØ Feedback Quality Guide for Responders

### If You're Asked for Feedback

**Do This:**

‚úÖ **Be Specific**
Include project names, situations, timeframes

‚úÖ **Describe Behaviors**
What they did, said, or created

‚úÖ **Explain Impact**
How their actions affected you, the team, or outcomes

‚úÖ **Balance Positive and Constructive**
Strengths + growth areas when appropriate

‚úÖ **Stay Factual**
Describe what you observed, not what you assume about their motivations

‚úÖ **Be Actionable**
Give feedback they can actually use

---

**Avoid This:**

‚ùå **Generic Statements**
"Great engineer" tells nothing

‚ùå **Personality Judgments**
Focus on behaviors, not character

‚ùå **Comparing to Others**
"Better than Alex" isn't helpful

‚ùå **Assumptions**
"They probably think..." Stick to what you observed

‚ùå **Punitive Tone**
Be constructive, not harsh
