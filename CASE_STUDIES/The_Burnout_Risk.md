# Case Study: The Burnout Risk

**The Problem:** High performer heading toward collapse

---

## üìã Background

**The IC:** Casey, a Senior Developer with 3 years at the company
**The Level:** Senior Developer (3.2/4.0) - genuinely strong
**The Issue:** Classic burnout trajectory - overwork, stress, beginning to show warning signs

**Specific Behaviors - Month 1:**
- Working 50-60 hour weeks consistently
- Shipping features faster than anyone on team
- Taking on extra projects ("someone has to")
- Staying late to help others ("I don't want to let them down")
- Skipping lunch, eating at desk
- Becoming irritable in meetings ("why is this so slow?")
- Code review comments getting sharp
- Started having trouble sleeping ("too much in my head")

**Team Perception:**
- "Casey is amazing, always delivers"
- "Casey got us out of this crisis"
- "Casey is the most reliable person"
- No one realizes Casey is drowning

**Project Context:**
- Team is chronically understaffed (2 people short for 8-month period)
- Company has hiring freeze
- Product demands high
- Casey essentially doing work of 1.5 people
- No one told Casey to stop or slow down

**Manager's Situation:**
- Manager is also stressed and overwhelmed
- Grateful for Casey's reliability
- Hasn't checked in deeply in months
- Realizes in hindsight: "I should have seen this"

---

## üîç Root Cause Analysis

**Why This Happened:**

1. **The Trap of Reliability**
   - Casey became "the reliable one"
   - Once you have that reputation, requests pile up
   - Saying no feels like letting people down
   - Casey's identity tied to being competent/helpful

2. **Systemic Problems**
   - Staffing shortage real but not solved
   - Company culture: work hard to solve problems
   - No protective mechanisms against overwork
   - Manager too busy to monitor workload

3. **Casey's Personality**
   - Perfectionist: doesn't hand off half-done work
   - Conscientious: feels responsible for team outcomes
   - Introverted: doesn't complain or ask for help
   - High standards: sees slack in others' work, takes it on

4. **Gradual Escalation**
   - Month 1: "helping out" on crisis
   - Month 2: became normal pace
   - Month 3: adding more projects
   - Month 4-5: no one questioning it
   - Month 6: Casey starting to show cracks

5. **No Circuit Breaker**
   - Manager should have intervened at month 2-3
   - Peers should have noticed
   - Company should have adjusted timeline or scope
   - None of that happened

---

## üí° Manager's Approach

### Phase 1: The Wake-Up Call (Week 1)

**What Triggered It:**

Casey sent email at 11 PM on a Sunday: "Working on the new feature, will have it done by morning"

Manager was horrified. Looked back at calendar and realized:
- Casey hasn't taken a day off in 6 weeks
- Working weekends for past month
- Recent Slack messages show deteriorating tone
- Code quality starting to slip (still high, but not Casey's usual)
- Casey missed 1:1 last week ("too busy")

**Manager's Setup:**

Called Casey, asked to talk in-person (not email/Slack)

"Casey, I need to say something important. I just realized I've let something happen that I shouldn't have. You're working unsustainable hours, and I didn't address it. That's on me."

**Manager Shared:**
- Looked at last 6 weeks of calendar/Slack
- "You're working 60-hour weeks"
- "You haven't taken a day off"
- "This isn't sustainable"
- "I'm concerned about your wellbeing"

**Casey's Response:**
- Initial: "I'm fine, these are just busy weeks"
- Then: "Someone has to get it done"
- Then: "Actually, I'm pretty tired" (opening up)

---

### Phase 2: Impact Assessment (Weeks 2-3)

**Manager and Casey Diagnosed Together:**

1. **Immediate Impact:**
   - Sleep disruption ("I can't shut off")
   - Irritability ("Everything's annoying me")
   - Cynicism ("Nothing matters anyway")
   - Reduced joy ("I used to enjoy coding; now it's just work")
   - Health neglect ("Haven't exercised in months")

2. **Workload Reality:**
   - Casey on 4 active projects (should be 1-2)
   - Covering for 2 open positions
   - "Helping out" on side requests (5+ per week)
   - Mentoring 2 junior devs
   - Code review queue never cleared

3. **What Casey Didn't Realize:**
   - Working slower when overworked (what seems like productivity is actually inefficiency)
   - Mistakes increasing (more PRs needing back-and-forth)
   - Not sustainable indefinitely (crisis burnout is visible collapse point)
   - Personal cost (health, relationships, mental health)

4. **Why Casey Didn't Speak Up:**
   - Felt responsible for team outcomes
   - Fear of being seen as "not a team player"
   - Perfectionism: couldn't hand off partially done work
   - Identity: "I'm the reliable one"

---

### Phase 3: Workload Reset (Week 3-4)

**Manager's Actions:**

1. **Scoped Down Projects:**
   - Pulled Casey off 2 projects (reassigned to others or deprioritized)
   - Kept Casey on 1-2 projects max
   - Made clear: "This is not optional"

2. **Stopped Side Requests:**
   - Manager became "gatekeeper"
   - When requests came for Casey: "Casey isn't available, here are alternatives"
   - Protected Casey's calendar

3. **Reassigned Work:**
   - Mentoring: paired junior devs, scaled back
   - Code review: distributed to team (not all to Casey)
   - "Side projects": assigned to others

4. **Set Boundaries:**
   - No Slack after 6 PM (manager enforced by not responding)
   - No weekend work (manager would call if it happened)
   - Core hours 10 AM - 4 PM (focus time protected)
   - Vacation: scheduled and non-negotiable

5. **Addressed Systemic Issue:**
   - Manager went to leadership: "We need to hire or reduce scope"
   - Hiring expedited for 2 open positions
   - Some commitments pushed to next quarter

**What Casey Had to Do:**

- Accept that projects might slip (hard for perfectionist)
- Trust others with work (hard when you like it done a certain way)
- Take time off (actually take it, not feel guilty)
- Go to sleep at reasonable hour (terrifying at first)
- Actually do vacation stuff (not check Slack)

---

### Phase 4: Recovery & Rebuilding (Weeks 4-12)

**Month 1 of Reset:**

- Casey working normal hours
- Sleeping better but still tired (takes time to recover)
- More alert, less irritable
- Work quality improving (less thrashing)
- Surprised: "Things are getting done without me"
- Eye-opening: "I was more of a bottleneck than a hero"

**Month 2-3:**

- Sleep normalized
- Energy returning
- Exercise resumed
- Perspective returning
- Acknowledges: "I was heading toward real collapse"

**Deep Conversation (Month 2):**

Manager and Casey discussed:

1. **The Pattern:**
   - "How did this happen?"
   - "What were the decision points?"
   - "When should you have spoken up?"
   - "When should I have intervened?"

2. **Casey's Patterns:**
   - Perfectionism as strength and vulnerability
   - Identity tied to reliability
   - Difficulty delegating
   - Fear of disappointing others
   - Not tracking own limits

3. **Going Forward:**
   - "What would make you feel safe speaking up?"
   - "How will we notice this pattern early?"
   - "What's your responsibility vs. manager's?"
   - Created early warning system

---

### Phase 5: Preventive Measures (Ongoing)

**New Practices Installed:**

1. **Workload Monitoring:**
   - Manager reviews Casey's project list monthly
   - Maximum 2 concurrent projects
   - No hidden work assignments
   - Peer review: "Is Casey overwhelmed?"

2. **Well-Being Check-Ins:**
   - Dedicated time in 1:1s for wellness
   - "How's your workload feeling?"
   - "When's your next day off?"
   - Non-judgmental, regular

3. **Team Load Balancing:**
   - Work distributed more fairly
   - No single person becomes bottleneck
   - New hires onboarded (positions finally filled)
   - Skills cross-trained

4. **Culture Shift (broader team):**
   - Manager modeled leaving at 6 PM
   - Celebrated sustainable pace
   - Normalized "I'm at capacity"
   - Made it team responsibility to notice overload

5. **Casey's Personal Changes:**
   - Got back to exercise routine
   - Set Slack boundaries (off at 6 PM)
   - Takes actual vacations
   - Practices saying "I'm at capacity"
   - More comfortable delegating

## üéì Key Learnings

### What Worked

1. **Manager Took Responsibility**
   - "This is on me" vs. "You should speak up"
   - Showed Casey it wasn't failure to need help
   - Manager became protector, not taskmaster

2. **Workload Cut Dramatically**
   - Not gradual (would've been undone)
   - Manager actively reassigned work
   - Made it clear: "This is non-negotiable"

3. **Addressed Systemic Issues**
   - Didn't just fix individual
   - Went to leadership for resources
   - Hired to close staffing gap
   - Prevented recurrence

4. **Recovery Time**
   - Didn't expect immediate bounce-back
   - Took months to fully recover
   - Normal that energy/joy return gradually
   - Patience with the process

5. **Team Adjustment**
   - Others stepped up when need was clear
   - Showed business didn't collapse without Casey taking everything
   - Helped team right-size expectations

### What Could Have Been Different

1. **Earlier Intervention**
   - Month 2 vs. Month 6
   - Would have prevented some damage
   - Manager should have been monitoring

2. **Prevention**
   - Better resource planning from start
   - No hiring freeze that created situation
   - Workload caps in company culture
   - Would have prevented entirely

3. **Casey's Part**
   - Earlier speaking up (though hard when pattern formed)
   - Setting own boundaries earlier
   - Asking for help
   - These skills could be coached

## üîÑ Alternative Scenarios

### If Casey Had Completely Burned Out

- Mental health crisis (burnout can trigger depression, anxiety)
- Medical leave needed (months off recovery)
- Permanent damage to retention
- Company loses senior engineer
- FMLA / disability / serious consequences

- Lost good employee
- Replacement costs (6+ months to rebuild)

- Signal to others about sustainability
- Could have been prevented

- Casey's collapse would have been dramatic
- Last-minute crisis intervention
- Much harder to recover
- Organizational reputation damage
- Legal/HR implications if severity high

## üíº For Your Team

**Early Warning Signs of Burnout:**

- Consistently working 50+ hours
- Skipping meals, breaks, days off
- Sleep disruption
- Irritability, cynicism, reduced patience
- Disengagement or perfectionism
- Health neglect
- Reduced quality (despite hard work)
- Becoming bottleneck for team

**Prevention Strategies:**

- Monitor workload regularly (not waiting for crisis)
- Enforce time-off and boundaries
- Hire adequately (don't expect people to cover shortages)
- Distribute work fairly
- Make sustainability a cultural value
- Manager models healthy boundaries

**Intervention Tactics:**

- Address immediately (this isn't a wait-and-see issue)
- Take responsibility as manager (don't blame person)
- Reduce workload dramatically (not incrementally)
- Address systemic issues (not just individual)
- Support recovery (patience, flexibility)
- Install preventive measures

**When to Escalate:**

- If person shows signs of depression/anxiety
- If health is significantly impacted
- If person considering leaving due to burnout
- If burnout-related medical issues emerge
- Employee Assistance Program (EAP) resources available
- HR/occupational health involvement if severe
