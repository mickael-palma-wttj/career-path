### 1Ô∏è‚É£ The Management Question
**Duration:** ~15 min | **[Read: When an IC Asks About Management](./The_Management_Question.md)**

**The Challenge:** "I'm thinking about becoming a manager. Can we talk about what that path looks like?"

**Character:** Priya Sharma, Senior Developer (Level 3), 3 years at level, strong performer, good at mentoring

**The Situation:**
Priya feels restless at Senior and sees peers getting promoted to Staff (unclear path). She's attracted to management because she loves mentoring and wants broader impact. But is she genuinely called to management, or just looking for a way to advance?

**Manager's Dilemma:**
Is she serious? Would she be good at it? Do we have opportunity? How do we not lose a strong IC?

**What Happens:**
Jessica creates a 3-month exploration where Priya tests management skills, explores the Staff IC path, and reflects weekly on what energizes vs. drains her.

**The Outcome:**
Priya discovers she wants the Staff IC path. Strategic product work energizes her; people problems drain her. She chooses Staff engineer path (product-focused archetype).

**What You'll Learn:**
- How to reality-check management without discouraging people
- Distinguish between "running away from IC" vs. "running toward management"
- Create low-risk experiments to test different paths
- Identify red flags that management might not be a good fit
- Support ICs exploring career options
- Structured 3-month exploration framework with decision tree

**Best For:** Managers who have ICs asking about management; ICs considering management; anyone exploring the IC vs. management decision

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ The Comeback Story
**Duration:** ~12 min | **[Read: Supporting Your Returning High Performer](./The_Comeback_Story.md)**

**The Challenge:** High performer returning from leave showing signs of struggle and disengagement

**Character:** Sam, Senior Developer returning from 6-month parental leave, previously strong performer

**The Situation:**
Sam took genuine leave (didn't work), came back to a changed codebase, new team members, and architectural decisions made without them. Feeling overwhelmed, uncertain, guilty, tired, anxious. Manager notices Sam withdrawn, not participating, taking on simple tasks, visibly less confident.

**Manager's Dilemma:**
Sam was a strong performer. What changed? Is this normal? How do we help without making it worse?

**What Happens:**
Manager recognizes it's not just "normal adjustment." Creates deliberate re-entry plan: structured knowledge transfer, pairing sessions, confidence-building through progressive challenge, celebration of return, rebuilding identity.

**The Outcome:**
Sam regains confidence, reconnects with team, rebuilds technical knowledge, returns to high performance. Learns that transitions after leave need intentional support, not just "welcome back."

**What You'll Learn:**
- How to recognize normal transition struggles vs. larger problems
- Create deliberate re-entry plans for returning employees
- Build confidence after significant life changes
- Use pairing and mentoring strategically during transitions
- Celebrate returns and recognize transition efforts
- Prevent disengagement during vulnerable periods

**Best For:** Managers supporting returning employees; anyone managing major life transitions; building compassionate return-to-work practices

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ The High Performer with Low Soft Skills
**Duration:** ~10 min | **[Read: Coaching Brilliant-but-Difficult Engineers](./The_High_Performer_with_Low_Soft_Skills.md)**

**The Challenge:** Technical genius, collaboration disaster. Brilliant coder destroying team morale.

**Character:** Alex, Senior Developer with 6 years at company, 3.5+ on technical criteria but 2.8/4.0 overall

**The Situation:**
Writes brilliant code that's hard to understand. Dismisses other approaches. Isolates from team. One-word answers. Made junior developer cry in code review. Skips meetings and feedback conversations. Team morale declining despite project success.

**Manager's Dilemma:**
Alex is technically exceptional but toxic to team. How do we address soft skills without losing technical talent? How do we make Alex see impact on others?

**What Happens:**
Manager has honest conversation with specific examples (junior dev's experience, team feedback, knowledge silos). Works together to diagnose strengths and growth areas. Creates collaborative learning plan: mentoring, code review guidelines, team participation, feedback loops.

**The Outcome:**
Alex starts to see impact on others. Recognizes that "Senior" means helping others succeed, not just doing hard technical work. Begins changing behavior through coaching, mentoring, and accountability. Becomes both technically strong AND collaborative.

**What You'll Learn:**
- How to give hard feedback about soft skills to technical performers
- Address defensive postures and resistance to feedback
- Help high performers understand Senior role expectations
- Create accountability for behavioral change
- Balance honoring technical excellence while addressing collaboration gaps
- Use specific examples and peer feedback effectively

**Best For:** Managers of brilliant-but-difficult ICs; addressing soft skill gaps; coaching high performers on collaboration; resetting role expectations

### 4Ô∏è‚É£ The Stuck Senior Developer
**Duration:** ~12 min | **[Read: Growth Beyond Promotion](./The_Stuck_Senior_Developer.md)**

**The Challenge:** Plateau at Senior level. Not ready for Staff. Frustrated with lack of growth.

**Character:** Jordan, Senior Developer for 4+ years, consistent performer, feeling plateau and frustration

**The Situation:**
Jordan has been Senior for years. Not ready for Staff (missing key skills/impact). Not interested in management. Feeling stuck, wondering if they're at career ceiling. Frustrated that promotion path is unclear. Energy declining, risk of disengagement or departure.

**Manager's Dilemma:**
How do we keep high performers engaged when they're not on track for promotion? How do we help them see growth opportunities that aren't "up"?

**What Happens:**
Manager helps Jordan see that not all growth is vertical. Explores lateral growth, specialization paths, skill development, expanded impact. Creates meaningful development plan focused on mastery rather than promotion. Celebrates expertise and creates higher-impact work.

**The Outcome:**
Jordan finds renewed purpose through skill development and expanded impact. No promotion but meaningful growth. Becomes go-to expert in specific areas. Renewed engagement and satisfaction.

**What You'll Learn:**
- Recognize plateau moments and intervening early
- Help ICs see non-vertical growth paths
- Distinguish between "not ready yet" and "not the right path"
- Create meaningful work for ICs not pursuing promotion
- Prevent disengagement during plateau periods
- Support specialization and expertise development

**Best For:** Managers of high performers who plateau; addressing stagnation and disengagement; creating lateral growth paths; retention during stagnation

### 5Ô∏è‚É£ Brilliant but Difficult
**Duration:** ~11 min | **[Read: When High Value Comes with High Cost](./Brilliant_but_Difficult.md)**

**The Challenge:** High-value contributor with problematic interpersonal behaviors. Attracts drama.

**Character:** Casey, strong technical contributor but frequently in conflict, creates tension wherever they go

**The Situation:**
Casey delivers strong technical work but constantly in interpersonal conflict. Tensions with managers, peers, and reports. Always their side of story‚Äîothers are wrong/incompetent/unfair. Drama follows them between projects and teams. Technically valuable but team cost is high.

**Manager's Dilemma:**
How do we value technical contribution while addressing pattern of conflict? How do we help Casey see their role in dynamics without losing their technical value?

**What Happens:**
Manager provides clear feedback about pattern (not just individual incidents). Uses 360 feedback to help Casey see impact across relationships. Moves Casey to role with less interpersonal complexity while working on self-awareness and skills. Sets clear expectations and consequences.

**The Outcome:**
Casey recognizes pattern and makes conscious effort to change. Not perfect but measurable improvement. Team relief visible. Casey becomes more self-aware about triggers and impact. More effective in role. Growth in emotional intelligence.

**What You'll Learn:**
- Distinguish between "this one incident" and "pattern of behavior"
- Use 360 feedback effectively to build self-awareness
- Address interpersonal patterns directly and compassionately
- Set clear expectations and boundaries around behavior
- Know when to move someone to different context
- Balance retaining talent with protecting team culture

**Best For:** Managers of high-value, difficult contributors; addressing interpersonal patterns; using 360 feedback; team culture protection

### 6Ô∏è‚É£ The Burnout Risk
**Duration:** ~13 min | **[Read: Recognizing and Preventing Burnout](./The_Burnout_Risk.md)**

**The Challenge:** High performer on path to burnout. Overcommitting, not setting boundaries, declining health.

**Character:** Morgan, strong contributor working excessive hours, taking on too much, showing warning signs

**The Situation:**
Morgan is a high performer known for delivery. Takes on too much. Works evenings and weekends. Skips exercise and sleep. Declining health and energy. Snapping at colleagues. Manager hasn't noticed until it's late stage. Risk of burnout, health crisis, or departure.

**Manager's Dilemma:**
Why didn't I notice earlier? How do I help Morgan recognize unsustainability? How do we reduce workload without seeming to demote? How do I protect Morgan without being overbearing?

**What Happens:**
Manager has caring conversation about sustainability (not performance). Acknowledges that current pace isn't sustainable. Works together to identify what can be dropped, delayed, or distributed. Sets clear boundaries on hours. Normalizes saying no. Creates accountability for rest and boundaries.

**The Outcome:**
Morgan scales back workload, sets boundaries, rebuilds health. Recognizes that saying no is strength, not weakness. Continues contributing at high level but sustainably. Grateful for manager's intervention before crisis.

**What You'll Learn:**
- Recognize early burnout warning signs
- Have conversations about sustainability vs. performance
- Help high performers set boundaries without shame
- Normalize saying no and incomplete work
- Create workload adjustment without signaling demotion
- Build accountability for rest and recovery

**Best For:** Managers supporting overcommitted high performers; preventing burnout; setting healthy boundaries; supporting wellness; early intervention

### 7Ô∏è‚É£ The Imposter Syndrome Trap
**Duration:** ~11 min | **[Read: Building Confidence in High-Doubters](./The_Imposter_Syndrome_Trap.md)**

**The Challenge:** Competent performer sabotaging themselves through self-doubt and perfectionism.

**Character:** Taylor, strong performer but discounts accomplishments, afraid of being "found out," perfectionistic

**The Situation:**
Taylor is genuinely skilled and performs well but attributes success to luck. Attributes failures to personal incompetence. Afraid of visibility and speaking up. Won't apply for stretch roles because "not ready yet." Undermines own confidence despite evidence of competence. Blocked by internal doubts rather than external barriers.

**Manager's Dilemma:**
How do I help Taylor see their actual competence? How do I build confidence without seeming condescending? How do I counter their own negative self-talk?

**What Happens:**
Manager uses evidence-based approach: reflects back actual accomplishments, has Taylor track successes, gathers feedback showing competence, challenges catastrophizing thinking, normalizes imposter feelings. Over time, builds evidence of competence that Taylor can't dismiss.

**The Outcome:**
Taylor gradually internalizes competence. Becomes willing to take on stretch assignments. Shares ideas more openly. Applies for promotion. Still has self-doubt moments but doesn't let them dictate decisions. More confident and visible.

**What You'll Learn:**
- Recognize imposter syndrome patterns vs. actual capability gaps
- Use evidence-based feedback to counter self-doubt
- Have conversations about perfectionism and self-sabotage
- Normalize imposter feelings while building actual confidence
- Coach through catastrophizing and what-if thinking
- Help ICs take visibility and stretch assignments despite self-doubt

**Best For:** Managers supporting self-doubting performers; building confidence; addressing perfectionism; pushing people toward deserved visibility

### 8Ô∏è‚É£ Promoted Too Early
**Duration:** ~14 min | **[Read: Supporting the Newly Promoted](./Promoted_Too_Early.md)**

**The Challenge:** Promoted before ready. Now struggling in new role. Risk of failure and loss of confidence.

**Character:** Devon, promoted to next level before fully ready, now overwhelmed in new role

**The Situation:**
Devon was strong at previous level, got promoted based on potential and business need. Now in new role feeling out of depth. Making mistakes. Questioning if promotion was deserved. Manager worried about poor performance. Risk that Devon loses confidence or fails in role.

**Manager's Dilemma:**
Did we promote too early? Should we demote? How do we support Devon without making failure more likely? How do we distinguish between "normal adjustment" and "wrong level"?

**What Happens:**
Manager assesses: Is this normal adjustment curve or real mismatch? Creates intensive support plan (mentoring, skill-building, realistic expectations, graduated responsibility). Sets clear success criteria and timeline. Checks in frequently. Provides coaching and feedback. Builds confidence through small wins.

**The Outcome:**
Devon adjusts to new level with support. Makes mistakes but learns. Gradually builds competence and confidence. Becomes solid performer at new level. Grateful for intensive support and belief from manager.

**What You'll Learn:**
- Distinguish between "normal adjustment struggle" and "wrong level"
- Create intensive support plans for early-promoted people
- Set realistic expectations for ramp time at new level
- Build confidence through small wins and recognition
- Provide skill-building and mentoring for gaps
- Know when to demote vs. support

**Best For:** Managers of recently promoted ICs; supporting adjustment to new level; deciding to support vs. demote; intensive mentoring; building competence through graduated responsibility

### 9Ô∏è‚É£ The Retention Crisis
**Duration:** ~12 min | **[Read: Understanding and Addressing Departures](./The_Retention_Crisis.md)**

**The Challenge:** Team members leaving or about to leave. What's driving departures? How do we retain?

**Character:** Multiple ICs at different levels, showing signs of disengagement or about to leave

**The Situation:**
Manager notices pattern: high performers looking elsewhere, reduced engagement, people saying "I'm exploring options." Root causes vary: unclear career path, feeling undervalued, compensation gaps, lack of meaningful work, better opportunities elsewhere. Risk of domino departures.

**Manager's Dilemma:**
How do I find out why people are really leaving? How do I differentiate between "normal job exploration" and "likely to leave"? How do I intervene? What can I actually retain people with?

**What Happens:**
Manager proactively has conversations (not interrogations) with people. Asks genuine questions about satisfaction, growth, what would make them want to stay. Listens without defensiveness. Addresses addressable concerns (clarity, meaning, development, recognition). Lets go of people for whom conditions can't be changed.

**The Outcome:**
Some people stay because concerns are addressed. Some people leave but feel valued and supported. Team culture improves because it's honest. Manager learns what drives retention in their specific context. Proactive approach prevents surprise departures.

**What You'll Learn:**
- Recognize early warning signs of disengagement and departure
- Have proactive retention conversations
- Distinguish between normal job exploration and likely departure
- Address addressable retention factors (clarity, meaning, development, recognition, compensation)
- Support people leaving with grace and goodwill
- Know what you can and can't control for retention
- Build culture where people feel heard and valued

**Best For:** Managers experiencing departures; retention and engagement; proactive career conversations; supporting people through job exploration

### üîü The Equity Gap
**Duration:** ~13 min | **[Read: Creating Equitable Career Development](./The_Equity_Gap.md)**

**The Challenge:** Inequitable treatment of similar performers. Women and underrepresented minorities advancing slower.

**Character:** Multiple ICs with similar performance but different outcomes based on identity/background

**The Situation:**
Manager notices pattern: Women and underrepresented minorities at same level advancing more slowly. Getting stretch assignments less frequently. Feedback patterns different (technical for men, soft skills for women). Visibility and recognition unequal. Same performance, different outcomes.

**Manager's Dilemma:**
Is this real or perception? Am I contributing to this? What's driving these differences? How do I address systematic inequities? How do I not make it weird?

**What Happens:**
Manager actively examines patterns with data. Gets feedback from affected people. Acknowledges patterns exist and they contribute. Makes deliberate changes: transparent stretch assignments, consistent feedback criteria, deliberate visibility creation, sponsorship for underrepresented performers, ongoing learning about bias.

**The Outcome:**
Advancement rates become more equitable. Different identity groups advance at similar rates for similar performance. Culture becomes more inclusive because it's intentional. Team performance improves with more diverse leadership. People feel seen and supported.

**What You'll Learn:**
- Recognize equity and advancement gaps
- Examine own biases and contribution to patterns
- Gather data and listen to affected people
- Make deliberate changes in: opportunities, feedback, visibility, sponsorship
- Create accountability for equity in career development
- Support underrepresented performers without tokenism
- Build inclusive culture that benefits everyone

**Best For:** Managers working on diversity and inclusion; addressing advancement gaps; recognizing and addressing bias; ensuring equitable career development

## üí° Common Themes Across All Cases

### 1. **Early Recognition**
Best managers notice patterns early, before situations become crises. Don't wait until it's too late.

### 2. **Caring Conversations**
Difficult topics require genuine care and curiosity, not judgment or agenda-pushing.

### 3. **Specific Feedback**
"You're not doing well" doesn't help. "Here's specific impact I'm observing" does.

### 4. **Collaborative Problem-Solving**
People are more likely to change when involved in diagnosing and solving their own situation.

### 5. **Creating Experiments**
Rather than "fix this," try "let's test this approach and see what happens."

### 6. **Clear Expectations**
People need to know what success looks like and how you'll measure it.

### 7. **Consistent Follow-Up**
One conversation doesn't change anything. Consistent, frequent check-ins do.

### 8. **Honesty About Constraints**
Be clear about what you can and can't control or change.

### 9. **Supporting the Path They Choose**
Even if they choose differently than you hoped, support their choice.

### 10. **Protecting Culture**
Individual situations matter, but protecting overall team culture is also crucial.

Have a real career development scenario you'd like to document? Great! Case studies should include:

1. **Situation Overview** - Character, context, what's happening
2. **The Challenge** - What makes this situation difficult?
3. **Initial Context** - Profiles, history, motivations, concerns
4. **The Conversation** - Real dialogue showing how to navigate
5. **Key Decision Points** - Where things could go different directions
6. **The Journey** - How situation evolved over time (if multi-stage)
7. **Resolution** - What happened and why
8. **Key Lessons** - What did the manager do right? Red flags?
9. **Alternative Outcomes** - What could have happened differently?
10. **Dialogue Examples** - Reusable language for similar conversations
11. **What You'll Learn** - Takeaways for readers
12. **Best For** - Who should read this case
