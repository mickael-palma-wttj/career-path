# Case Study: The Management Question

Navigating conversations when an IC asks about switching to engineering management.

---

## üìä Case Overview

**Developer:** Priya Sharma (name changed)  
**Level:** Senior Developer (Level 3)  
**Time at Level:** 3 years  
**Company Size:** ~120 engineers  
**Team:** Mobile Platform  
**Manager:** Jessica (Engineering Manager, 2.5 years managing Priya)

**The Question:**
"I'm thinking about becoming a manager. Can we talk about what that path looks like?"

---

## üéØ Initial Situation

### Priya's Profile

**Strong Performer:**
- Solid Senior Developer, consistently "Meets/Exceeds Expectations"
- Good technical skills and judgment
- Mentors 2 Confirmed developers effectively
- Well-respected by team
- Good communicator and collaborator

**Recent Context:**
- Feeling restless at Senior level (3 years)
- Sees peers getting promoted to Staff, but that path unclear
- Recently attended "Intro to Engineering Management" workshop
- Observes Jessica's work and thinks "I could do that"
- Motivated by helping people grow

---

### Jessica's Initial Reaction

**Internal Questions:**
- Is Priya serious or just exploring?
- Would Priya be a good manager?
- Do we have a management opportunity?
- How do I not lose a strong IC?
- What if they try management and hate it?

**Jessica's Approach:**
Don't immediately say yes or no‚Äîhave a real conversation to understand motivations and assess fit.

---

## üí¨ The Conversation

### Meeting 1: Understanding Motivations (Week 1)

**Jessica:** "Thanks for bringing this up. I'm glad you're thinking about your career. Tell me more about what's making you consider management."

**Priya:** "I've been at Senior for 3 years, and I'm not sure Staff is the right path for me. I really enjoy mentoring‚Äîit's the most rewarding part of my work. I see you helping people grow and think I'd be good at that."

**Jessica:** "What specifically appeals to you about management?"

**Priya's Reasons:**
- ‚úÖ "I love helping people develop their skills"
- ‚úÖ "I want to have broader impact beyond my own code"
- ‚úÖ "I'm good at facilitating discussions and resolving conflicts"
- ‚úÖ "I want to help shape team direction"
- üö© "I feel stuck at Senior and not sure how else to advance"
- üö© "It seems like managers have more influence and respect"

**Jessica:** "Those are interesting motivations. What do you think managers actually do day-to-day?"

**Priya:** "Well... you have 1:1s, you help people with their careers, you go to planning meetings... I guess I'm not totally sure about all of it?"

**Jessica's Observation:**
Good motivations (helping people) mixed with concerning ones (feeling stuck, wanting status). Priya doesn't fully understand what management entails.

---

**Jessica:** "Let me share what my actual days look like..."

**Jessica's Reality Check:**

**What Management Actually Is:**
- 8-10 hours of meetings per week (1:1s, planning, coordination)
- Handling performance issues (difficult conversations)
- Dealing with ambiguity and politics
- Much less coding (I code ~5 hours/week vs. 30+ when I was IC)
- Responsibility for others' work (you don't write code, you enable others to)
- Being "on" emotionally‚Äîpeople need you
- Firefighting and unblocking constantly
- Administrative work (reviews, planning, budget)

**What You Give Up:**
- Deep technical work and flow state
- Control over your own work
- Quick wins (management impact is slow)
- Clear success metrics (management is fuzzy)
- Technical skill development (atrophies without effort)

**Priya:** "Oh. I didn't realize you code so little. That would be hard."

**Jessica:** "Yeah, it's a real shift. The hardest part for me was giving up solving technical problems directly. Now I solve people problems."

---

**Jessica:** "Here's what I want you to do: For the next two weeks, pay attention to what actually energizes you. Is it the technical problem-solving? The people interactions? The strategy discussions? Let's talk again after you've reflected."

**Homework:**
- Shadow Jessica for 4 hours (see real management work)
- Read: "The Manager's Path" (chapters 1-3)
- Talk to 2 other managers about their experience
- Reflect on: What energizes me? What drains me?

---

### Meeting 2: Reality Check (Week 3)

**Jessica:** "How did the shadowing and conversations go?"

**Priya:** "It was eye-opening. I saw you handle a performance conversation that looked really draining. And the amount of context-switching is intense‚Äîyou went from technical decision to HR discussion to roadmap planning in like 30 minutes."

**Jessica:** "Yeah, that's pretty typical. What did you think?"

**Priya:** "Honestly... it looked hard. But also interesting. The performance conversation was uncomfortable, but I could see how important it was for the person. And I liked seeing you help Tom unblock his project."

**Jessica:** "What did the other managers tell you?"

**Priya's Learnings:**
- "Sarah said the first year is really hard‚Äîlearning curve is steep"
- "Mike said he misses coding and has to do side projects to scratch that itch"
- "Both said it's rewarding but totally different from IC work"
- "Sarah warned me not to do it if I'm running away from IC‚Äîdo it because I'm running toward management"

---

**Jessica:** "That last point is crucial. Let's talk about your IC path. You said you feel stuck at Senior. Have we explored what Staff level could look like for you?"

**Priya:** "Not really. Staff seems like it's all architecture and I'm more product-focused."

**Jessica:** "Actually, there are multiple Staff archetypes. Some Staff engineers are deeply product-focused, working cross-functionally. That could be a fit. Would you want to explore that path before deciding on management?"

**Critical Moment:**
Is Priya considering management because she genuinely wants to manage people, or because she doesn't see another advancement path?

**Priya:** [Pause] "I think I should understand the Staff path better. But I'm still interested in management, especially long-term."

**Jessica:** "Here's what I suggest: Let's spend 3 months exploring the Staff path while also giving you some management-adjacent experience. Then you'll have better information to decide."

---

## üí° The Development Plan

### 3-Month Exploration Plan

**Goal:** Give Priya real data about both paths before deciding.

---

**Track 1: Understand Staff IC Path**

**Activities:**
- Review [Staff Developer Guide](../CAREER_LEVEL_GUIDES/04_Staff_Developer.md)
- Shadow 2 Staff engineers for a day each (different archetypes)
- Lead 1 cross-team technical initiative
- Attend Staff engineer panel discussion
- Identify what Staff-level work interests her

**Timeline:** Month 1-3

---

**Track 2: Test Management Skills**

**Activities:**
- **Mentorship Expansion:**
  - Take on 1 additional mentee (3 total)
  - Run structured mentoring with goals and plans
  - Practice career development conversations

- **Team Leadership:**
  - Lead team retro for 3 months
  - Run incident post-mortem
  - Facilitate technical decision-making meeting

- **People Processes:**
  - Participate in interview panel (hiring)
  - Shadow Jessica during performance review calibration
  - Help with junior engineer's development plan

- **Difficult Conversations:**
  - Role-play performance conversation with Jessica
  - Deliver constructive feedback to peer (with coaching)

**Timeline:** Month 1-3

---

**Track 3: Self-Assessment**

**Weekly Reflection Questions:**
- What energized me this week?
- What drained me?
- Did I miss deep technical work?
- Did I enjoy the people-focused work?
- Am I excited or anxious about management tasks?

**Timeline:** Every Friday for 12 weeks

---

## üìà The Journey: 3-Month Exploration

### Month 1: Discovery

**Staff IC Path Exploration:**

Priya shadowed two Staff engineers:
- **David (Tech Lead archetype):** Deep in technical decisions, coding 60% of time
- **Lisa (Product Engineer archetype):** Cross-functional work, strategy, less coding

**Priya's Reaction:**
"I really liked Lisa's work‚Äîshaping product direction, working with PMs and designers, still technical but more strategic. That was cool. David's role felt too deep in the weeds for me."

**Priya's Insight:** "I didn't know Staff could be product-focused like Lisa. That's interesting."

---

**Management-Adjacent Work:**

**Mentoring Expansion:**
Added mentee #3 (Confirmed developer, Maria). Ran structured career conversations.

**Challenge:** Maria struggling with confidence, needed emotional support not just technical guidance.

**Priya's Reflection:**
"This was harder than I thought. Maria needed encouragement and I wasn't sure how to give it. I wanted to fix her technical problems, but that's not what she needed."

**Jessica's Coaching:**
"Management is often about helping people solve their own problems, not solving for them. It's uncomfortable."

---

### Month 2: Testing

**Staff IC Project:**
Led cross-team initiative to standardize mobile analytics.

**The Experience:**
- Technical strategy and decision-making
- Coordinating 3 teams
- Influencing without authority
- Balancing competing priorities

**Priya's Reaction:**
"This was really satisfying. I liked the strategic thinking and seeing my work affect multiple teams. The politics were annoying but manageable."

---

**Management Work:**

**Led Team Retro:**
Prepared agenda, facilitated discussion, handled conflict.

**Challenge:** Two engineers disagreed on process change. Priya had to navigate tension.

**Priya's Reflection:**
"I thought retros would be easy‚Äîthey're not. Reading the room, drawing out quiet people, managing strong opinions... that was exhausting. But we made good decisions."

**Shadowed Performance Calibration:**
Observed Jessica and other managers debate performance ratings.

**Priya's Reaction:**
"This was intense. Advocating for your people while being fair across teams is really hard. And some of the decisions felt political, not just performance-based. That bothered me."

---

### Month 3: Clarity Emerging

**Week 9: Key Realization**

**Priya's Journal:**
"I had a really energizing week. I helped Lisa design the mobile strategy for our new product‚Äîthat was so fun. I love thinking about 'what should we build and why.' But then I had 2 hours of 1:1s with my mentees. One was great (technical discussion), but one was emotional (mentee frustrated with career). That conversation drained me. I didn't know what to say."

**Pattern:** Strategic/product work energizes her. People problems drain her.

---

**Week 10: Difficult Conversation Practice**

**Role-play:** Priya practiced delivering feedback to underperforming engineer (Jessica played the IC).

**The Exercise:**
"Jordan, I need to talk about your recent work. The last two projects missed deadlines and the code quality wasn't up to our standards..."

**Priya's Experience:**
"I hated this. Even in role-play, I felt anxious and wanted to avoid confrontation. Jessica got defensive (intentionally) and I didn't know how to respond. I just wanted to escape."

**Jessica's Observation:**
"Priya's discomfort with conflict is real. She can learn these skills, but they don't come naturally. Management requires a LOT of difficult conversations."

---

**Week 12: Final Reflection**

**Priya and Jessica Debrief:**

**Jessica:** "You've explored both paths for 3 months. What have you learned?"

**Priya's Insights:**

**What Energized Her:**
- ‚úÖ Strategic product thinking (Staff-level IC work)
- ‚úÖ Cross-team collaboration
- ‚úÖ Technical mentoring (teaching concepts)
- ‚úÖ Influencing direction and decisions
- ‚úÖ Seeing her work have broad impact

**What Drained Her:**
- ‚ùå Emotional support conversations
- ‚ùå Performance management / difficult conversations
- ‚ùå Process discussions and facilitation
- ‚ùå Administrative work
- ‚ùå Conflict resolution

**Priya:** "I think I was confusing 'wanting impact' with 'wanting to be a manager.' I do want impact, but I realize I want it through strategic IC work, not through managing people."

**Jessica:** "What about the feeling of being stuck at Senior?"

**Priya:** "I didn't know Staff could look like what Lisa does. That's the path I want‚Äîproduct-focused Staff engineer. I want to shape what we build, not manage the people building it."

---

## üéØ The Decision

**Priya's Choice:** Pursue Staff IC path (product-focused archetype)

**New Development Plan:**
- Continue cross-team product-focused projects
- Develop product strategy skills
- Build relationships with Product/Design
- Lead technical product direction
- Work toward Staff promotion in 12-18 months

**Management Path:**
- Remain open to management in future (5+ years)
- "Maybe when I'm older and have more patience for people problems"
- "But right now, I want to do strategic IC work"

## üéì Key Lessons

### What Jessica Did Right

‚úÖ **Didn't Immediately Say Yes or No**
Created space for Priya to explore before committing.

‚úÖ **Reality-Checked Management**
Shared honest view of what management entails, not glamorized version.

‚úÖ **Identified Root Cause**
Realized Priya felt stuck, not necessarily called to management.

‚úÖ **Provided Experiments**
Let Priya test both paths with low-risk experiences.

‚úÖ **Coached Through Discovery**
Asked reflection questions, didn't tell Priya what to decide.

‚úÖ **Expanded IC Path Understanding**
Showed Priya that Staff doesn't mean one thing‚Äîmultiple archetypes exist.

‚úÖ **Supported Final Decision**
When Priya chose IC path, Jessica supported enthusiastically.

---

### Signals That Management Might Not Be Right

üö© **Avoiding problems, not running toward opportunity**
"I'm stuck" rather than "I'm excited to develop people"

üö© **Seeking status or advancement, not the work itself**
"Managers have more influence" rather than "I love people development"

üö© **Discomfort with conflict**
Strong aversion to difficult conversations (can be learned, but it's hard)

üö© **Energized by technical work, drained by people work**
If people interactions exhaust you, management will be brutal

üö© **Misunderstanding what management is**
Thinking it's "telling people what to do" vs. enabling, coaching, navigating ambiguity

---

### Signals That Management Might Be Right

‚úÖ **Energized by developing others**
Genuinely excited when mentees succeed

‚úÖ **Comfortable with ambiguity**
Thrives when problems are fuzzy, not just technical

‚úÖ **Enjoys facilitation and coordination**
Finds meetings productive, not draining

‚úÖ **Willing to give up technical depth**
Accepts coding will decrease significantly

‚úÖ **Patient with people problems**
Doesn't get frustrated when people need support

‚úÖ **Thinks strategically about people and teams**
Naturally considers "who should work on what" and "how do we build this team"

## üîÑ Alternative Outcomes

### Scenario A: Priya Decides She Wants Management

**If testing revealed genuine management interest:**

**Jessica's Next Steps:**
1. **Create management opportunity:**
   - Promote to Tech Lead role first (management-lite)
   - Manage 1-2 ICs initially
   - Keep some IC work (60% management / 40% IC)

2. **Provide training:**
   - Management fundamentals course
   - Pair with Jessica on management tasks
   - Connect with management community

3. **Set expectations:**
   - First year is learning curve
   - Performance may dip initially (new skills)
   - Reversible decision (can return to IC)

4. **Support transition:**
   - Weekly coaching with Jessica
   - Management peer group
   - Clear goals for first 90 days

**Timeline:** 3-6 months to transition

---

### Scenario B: No Management Opportunity Available

**If Priya wants management but no opening exists:**

**Jessica's Options:**

**Option 1: Create Opportunity**
- Split current team, Priya manages part
- Have Priya manage new hires
- Interim coverage when Jessica on leave

**Option 2: Prepare for Future Opportunity**
- Continue management-adjacent work
- Get on "ready for management" list
- When opportunity arises, Priya is first choice
- Timeline: 6-12 months

**Option 3: Help Priya Find Role Elsewhere**
- Internal transfer to team with opening
- External opportunity with Jessica's support
- Retain Priya as IC until opening exists

**Critical:** Don't promise management if there's no realistic path. Be honest about timeline and likelihood.

---

### Scenario C: Priya Was Wrong Fit for Management

**If testing revealed serious concerns:**

**Red flags might be:**
- Extreme discomfort with people issues
- No improvement despite coaching
- Feedback from mentees was negative
- Priya hated all management tasks

**Jessica's Approach:**
- Honest conversation: "Based on what we've seen, I'm not confident management is the right fit"
- Specific examples of concerns
- Focus energy on Staff IC path
- Keep door open for revisiting in future
- "Not right now doesn't mean not ever"

## üí¨ Dialogue Examples

### Reality-Checking Management

**Jessica:** "Before we go further, let me tell you about the hardest parts of my job that people don't see."

**Jessica:** "Last week, I had to tell someone they weren't getting promoted when they expected to. They cried in my office. Then I had a meeting where two directors disagreed about our roadmap and I had to navigate their politics. Then I spent 3 hours on performance reviews‚Äîadministrative work that felt like paperwork. And I coded for about 4 hours all week. That's management."

**Priya:** "That sounds... hard."

**Jessica:** "It is. The question is: does that sound hard but worthwhile, or hard and terrible? Because that's what you're signing up for."

---

### Exploring the Real Motivation

**Jessica:** "You mentioned feeling stuck at Senior. Tell me more about that."

**Priya:** "I just... I don't know what's next. I see people getting promoted to Staff but I don't know if that's my path."

**Jessica:** "What if we figured out a Staff path that excites you? Would you still want management?"

**Priya:** "I'm not sure. Maybe not?"

**Jessica:** "That's useful information. Let's make sure you're choosing management because you want it, not because you don't see another option."

---

### Supporting the Final Decision

**Jessica:** "So after 3 months of exploring, what's your sense?"

**Priya:** "I think I want the Staff IC path. The product-focused work energizes me. Management... I respect it, but it's not for me right now."

**Jessica:** "I think that's a great decision. You've tested both paths and made an informed choice. I'm excited to support your Staff journey. And management will still be an option in 5 years if you change your mind."

**Priya:** "Thanks for letting me explore. I feel confident now."

## üìä Decision Framework for Managers

### When an IC Asks About Management

**Step 1: Understand Motivation (Week 1)**
- Why are they considering management?
- What do they think managers do?
- Are they running away from IC or toward management?
- What energizes/drains them?

**Step 2: Reality Check (Week 1-2)**
- Share honest view of management
- Explain trade-offs
- Provide reading/shadowing
- Talk to other managers

**Step 3: Assess IC Path Clarity (Week 2-3)**
- Do they understand their IC advancement options?
- Have we explored all IC paths?
- Are they stuck or just uninformed?

**Step 4: Create Experiments (Month 1-3)**
- Test management skills (low-risk)
- Explore Staff IC path simultaneously
- Weekly reflections
- What energizes vs. drains?

**Step 5: Decision Conversation (Month 3)**
- Review learnings
- Discuss fit assessment
- Make informed choice
- Create plan for chosen path

---

### Your Assessment of Fit

**Consider:**
- Do they show management aptitude in experiments?
- Are they energized or drained by people work?
- Can they handle conflict and ambiguity?
- Are motivations healthy (helping others) or concerning (seeking status)?
- Would they be good for their reports?

**Your Honesty:**
If you don't think they'd be a good manager, say so kindly:
"Based on what I've observed, I'm not sure management is the right fit right now. Let me share my concerns..."
