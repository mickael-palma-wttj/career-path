## üß≠ Core Principles to Guide You

### 1. **Transparency Builds Trust**
Share this framework openly with your ICs. Let them see:
- What's expected at their level
- What's required for promotion
- Where they currently stand
- What specific areas to develop

**Why:** When people understand the game, they can play it better.

### 2. **Feedback Should Be Continuous, Not Annual**
Don't save feedback for reviews. Give it:
- **In the moment** - When you observe something
- **Specifically** - Tied to concrete behaviors
- **Bidirectionally** - Ask for feedback on your management too

**Why:** Real-time feedback creates faster learning loops.

### 3. **Evidence Over Opinions**
Base your assessments on:
- Observed behaviors and outcomes
- Peer feedback and collaboration patterns
- Artifacts (code, docs, presentations)
- Impact on projects and team

**Why:** Concrete evidence makes feedback actionable and reduces bias.

### 4. **Different Paths, Same Standards**
Some ICs will:
- Progress quickly through early levels
- Plateau at a level that matches their aspirations
- Move laterally to explore different strengths
- Take breaks in growth for personal reasons

**All of this is okay.** The standards remain consistent, but the journey is individual.

### 5. **Promotion Shouldn't Surprise Anyone**
If you're doing this right:
- ICs working toward promotion know it 6+ months in advance
- You've clearly articulated what "ready" looks like
- They've demonstrated readiness consistently
- The promotion decision validates what everyone already knows

**Why:** Surprises (positive or negative) mean communication failed.

## üí¨ Your First Career Conversation

### Opening
> "I'd like to spend time today talking about your career goals and how I can support you. I want to make sure we're aligned on where you want to go and what you need to get there. This framework is a tool to help us both be clear about expectations and progress."

### Key Questions to Ask

**Understanding Aspirations:**
- "Where do you see yourself in 2-3 years?"
- "What kind of work energizes you? What drains you?"
- "Are you interested in people management, or do you prefer the IC track?"
- "What does success look like for you in your career?"

**Current State Assessment:**
- "Looking at [their level], which areas do you feel strongest in?"
- "Which areas would you like to develop further?"
- "What projects or experiences have been most valuable for your growth?"
- "What obstacles are getting in the way of your development?"

**Creating Alignment:**
- "Based on your goals, here's what I'm seeing as priorities..."
- "Does that align with what you were thinking?"
- "What support do you need from me?"
- "How often would you like to discuss career progress?"

### Closing
> "I'm committed to helping you grow. Let's check in on this at least once a month. Between now and our next 1:1, I'll draft a development plan for us to review together. Feel free to come to me anytime with questions or if you want to discuss something specific."

## üöß Common Pitfalls for New Managers

### ‚ùå Waiting Until Review Time
**Don't:** Save all feedback for formal reviews.  
**Do:** Provide continuous, real-time feedback throughout the year.

### ‚ùå Being Vague
**Don't:** "You need to be more senior."  
**Do:** "To reach Senior level, I'd like to see you leading technical design discussions and mentoring junior developers. Let's work on getting you those opportunities."

### ‚ùå Making It About You
**Don't:** "I need you to be senior so my team looks stronger."  
**Do:** "What are your career goals, and how can I support you in achieving them?"

### ‚ùå Avoiding Difficult Conversations
**Don't:** Hope performance issues resolve themselves.  
**Do:** Address concerns early with specific examples and clear expectations.

### ‚ùå Promoting Too Early
**Don't:** Promote based on potential or to retain someone.  
**Do:** Promote when someone has consistently demonstrated readiness across all key areas.

### ‚ùå Comparing ICs to Each Other
**Don't:** "You're not as strong as Sarah at this."  
**Do:** "Based on the Senior Developer criteria, here's where I see opportunity for growth..."

## ü§î Frequently Asked Questions

### "How much time should I spend on career development?"

**Minimum:** 
- 30 min/month per IC for direct conversation
- 30 min/month per IC for planning and documentation
- Ongoing observation and feedback (integrated into daily work)

**Realistic:**
- 1 hour/month per IC for direct career work
- Plus informal coaching throughout the week

### "What if an IC doesn't want to progress?"

That's completely valid! Some people are happy at their current level. Your job is to:
- Ensure they maintain performance at that level
- Keep them engaged and learning (even if not pursuing promotion)
- Revisit career goals periodically in case aspirations change
- Support them in deepening expertise at their level

### "What if I don't have opportunities for someone to grow?"

This is a real challenge. Consider:
- Cross-team projects or collaborations
- Temporary assignments to different areas
- Creating new initiatives they can lead
- External learning (conferences, courses, side projects)
- If truly limited, be honest about timing and constraints

### "How do I handle someone who thinks they're ready but isn't?"

- Use the framework to point to specific gaps
- Provide concrete examples of what "ready" looks like
- Show examples of behaviors you're looking for
- Create a clear development plan with milestones
- Check for calibration with other managers
- Be direct but supportive: "Here's the gap, here's how we close it"

### "What if I disagree with how someone was leveled before I arrived?"

- Focus on current state and forward progress
- If you believe they're mis-leveled, gather evidence
- Discuss with your leadership and HR
- If you can't change the level, focus on helping them grow into it or beyond it
- Be transparent with the IC about the situation
