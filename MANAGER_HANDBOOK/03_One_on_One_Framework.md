# One-on-One Framework: Career Development Focus

A comprehensive guide to conducting effective 1:1 meetings that drive Individual Contributor career growth.

---

## üéØ Why 1:1s Matter

One-on-ones are your **most important tool** as a manager for:
- Building trust and psychological safety
- Understanding your IC's aspirations and concerns
- Providing regular feedback and coaching
- Identifying and removing blockers
- Tracking career progress
- Catching issues early

**When done well:** ICs feel supported, grow faster, and stay longer.  
**When done poorly:** ICs feel neglected, plateau, and leave.

---

## üìÖ Frequency & Duration

### Recommended Cadence

| IC Level | Frequency | Duration | Career Focus |
|----------|-----------|----------|--------------|
| **Junior** | Weekly | 30-45 min | Every other week |
| **Confirmed** | Weekly or Bi-weekly | 30 min | Monthly |
| **Senior** | Bi-weekly | 30 min | Monthly |
| **Staff+** | Bi-weekly | 30 min | Bi-monthly |

**Adjust based on:**
- Individual needs and preferences
- Current projects and challenges
- Active development periods
- Performance concerns
- Remote vs. co-located

### Sacred Time Principle

**1:1s should be sacred‚Äîdon't cancel them.**

If you absolutely must reschedle:
- ‚úÖ Give advance notice
- ‚úÖ Reschedule immediately
- ‚úÖ Explain why (briefly)
- ‚úÖ Make it the exception, not the rule

**Warning signs:**
- You've cancelled 3+ times in 2 months
- ICs stop bringing up important topics
- Relationship feels transactional
- Trust is eroding

## üèóÔ∏è Structure & Agenda

### The 70/30 Rule

**They talk 70% of the time, you talk 30%.**

This is **their meeting**, not yours. Your job is to:
- Listen actively
- Ask clarifying questions
- Provide guidance when asked
- Give feedback when appropriate
- Remove blockers

### Basic Meeting Structure

#### 1. Opening (2-3 min)
- Check-in on mood/energy
- Review action items from last time
- Set agenda for today

#### 2. Their Topics (50-60%)
- What's on their mind
- Challenges they're facing
- Questions they have
- Ideas they want to discuss

#### 3. Your Topics (30-40%)
- Feedback you have
- Context to share
- Career development
- Team/org updates

#### 4. Closing (2-3 min)
- Summarize key points
- Confirm action items
- Schedule next discussion if needed

## üí¨ Career Development Integration

### Monthly Career-Focused 1:1

**Dedicate one 1:1 per month primarily to career development.**

#### Structure for Career 1:1 (45-60 min)

**Part 1: Progress Review (15 min)**
- Review development goals
- Discuss recent wins and challenges
- Assess growth trajectory

**Part 2: Feedback Exchange (15 min)**
- Your feedback on recent work
- Their self-assessment
- Peer feedback summary if collected

**Part 3: Forward Planning (15 min)**
- Upcoming opportunities
- Skill development focus
- Goal refinement

**Part 4: Action Items (5 min)**
- Clear next steps
- Timeline commitments

### Weekly Career Touch-points

Even in weekly 1:1s, include brief career elements:

**5-Minute Career Check:**
- "How are you feeling about your current work?"
- "Any development goals we should talk about?"
- "Anything you want to learn or try?"

## üé§ Powerful Questions to Ask

### Understanding Aspirations

**Long-term:**
- "Where do you see yourself in 2-3 years?"
- "What does success look like for you?"
- "What kind of impact do you want to have?"
- "Are you interested in management or staying IC?"

**Short-term:**
- "What do you want to learn this quarter?"
- "What experiences would be valuable for you?"
- "What projects excite you most?"

**Motivation:**
- "What parts of your work give you energy?"
- "What drains you?"
- "When do you feel most proud of your work?"
- "What would make this role more fulfilling?"

### Assessing Current State

**Performance:**
- "How do you feel you're performing at [criterion]?"
- "What's been going well recently?"
- "What's been challenging?"
- "Where do you feel strongest/weakest?"

**Growth:**
- "What have you learned in the past month?"
- "How are you tracking against your goals?"
- "What progress have you made on [skill]?"

**Blockers:**
- "What's getting in the way of your best work?"
- "What support do you need that you're not getting?"
- "What would help you move faster?"

### Giving Context

**Framework:**
- "Let me share how I'm seeing your performance..."
- "Here's where I see you relative to [level] expectations..."
- "Based on the framework, here's what I'm observing..."

**Expectations:**
- "At your level, we're looking for..."
- "To get to the next level, you'd need to demonstrate..."
- "Here's what 'good' looks like for this..."

**Opportunities:**
- "I'm seeing an opportunity for you to..."
- "This upcoming project would be a great fit because..."
- "Here's how this work aligns with your goals..."

### Coaching Questions

**Reflection:**
- "What do you think went well there?"
- "What would you do differently next time?"
- "What did you learn from that experience?"

**Problem-solving:**
- "What options have you considered?"
- "What do you think you should do?"
- "What's holding you back from trying that?"

**Growth:**
- "What would it look like if you improved at this?"
- "Who could you learn from?"
- "What resources would help?"

## üìù Note-Taking & Documentation

### Why Document?

- Track patterns over time
- Remember commitments
- Prepare for reviews
- Evidence for promotions
- Protect yourself legally

### What to Document

**‚úÖ Always Document:**
- Key discussion topics
- Action items (with owners and dates)
- Feedback given (positive and constructive)
- Career aspirations and goals
- Development plan updates
- Performance concerns
- Commitments made by you or them

**‚ö†Ô∏è Document Carefully:**
- Personal/sensitive information (keep appropriate)
- Health or family matters (only if relevant to work)
- Confidential information (follow company policy)

**‚ùå Don't Document:**
- Information they explicitly ask to keep off-record
- Speculation or assumptions
- Information that could be used against them unfairly

### Documentation Template

```markdown
## 1:1 with [Name] - [Date]

### Their Topics
- [Topic 1]: [Notes]
- [Topic 2]: [Notes]

### My Topics
- Feedback: [Specific feedback given]
- Context: [Information shared]

### Career Discussion
- Progress: [Summary]
- Goals: [Updates]
- Next focus: [Areas]

### Action Items
- [ ] [Name] will [action] by [date]
- [ ] [Manager] will [action] by [date]

### Follow-up
- [Items to revisit next time]

### Private Manager Notes
- [Observations, patterns, concerns]
```

## üéØ Making 1:1s Effective

### Preparation (Manager)

**Before Each Meeting:**
- [ ] Review notes from last 1:1
- [ ] Check action items completion
- [ ] Review recent work (PRs, projects)
- [ ] Prepare specific feedback examples
- [ ] Note any team/org updates to share
- [ ] Think about career development topics

**Time Investment:** 10-15 minutes

### Preparation (IC)

**Encourage them to prepare:**
- Topics they want to discuss
- Questions they have
- Updates on their goals
- Blockers or challenges
- Wins to share

**Share ahead of time:** Use a shared doc or Slack thread

### During the Meeting

**‚úÖ Do:**
- Start on time
- Be fully present (close laptop if not taking notes)
- Listen more than talk
- Ask open-ended questions
- Take notes
- Be honest and direct
- Show genuine interest

**‚ùå Don't:**
- Check your phone or email
- Make it all about status updates
- Do all the talking
- Be vague or generic
- Skip career discussions
- Rush through topics
- Make promises you can't keep

## üöß Handling Difficult Conversations

### When They're Underperforming

**Approach:**
1. **Be direct but kind:** "I need to talk about some concerns I have..."
2. **Be specific:** Use concrete examples with context
3. **Listen:** Understand their perspective
4. **Problem-solve together:** "What would help you improve?"
5. **Create a plan:** Clear expectations and timeline
6. **Follow up:** Regular check-ins on progress

**Example:**
> "I want to discuss the quality of recent PRs. In the last three pull requests [#123, #124, #125], there were significant bugs that made it to production despite code review. This is concerning because it impacts the team's velocity and customer experience. Help me understand what's happening from your perspective. What support do you need to improve the quality before submitting PRs?"

### When They're Unhappy/Disengaged

**Approach:**
1. **Notice and name it:** "You seem less engaged lately..."
2. **Create safety:** "I want to understand what's going on..."
3. **Ask open questions:** "What's changed?"
4. **Listen deeply:** Don't jump to solutions
5. **Explore together:** "What would make this better?"

**Example:**
> "I've noticed that in the past few weeks, you've seemed less engaged in team meetings and your energy feels different. I care about you and want to understand what's happening. Is there something going on with work, or is there a way I can better support you?"

### When They Want to Leave

**Approach:**
1. **Don't panic:** Stay calm and curious
2. **Understand why:** "What's prompting this?"
3. **Be genuine:** "I'd love to keep you if we can address what's wrong"
4. **Don't overpromise:** Only commit to what you can deliver
5. **Respect their decision:** If they've decided, support them

**Example:**
> "Thank you for telling me. I'm sad to hear this, but I appreciate your honesty. Help me understand what's driving this decision. Is there anything that would make you reconsider, or have you already made up your mind? If there are things we could address, I'd like to explore that, but I also respect your decision."

### When You Have to Deliver Bad News

**Approach:**
1. **Be direct:** Don't bury the lead
2. **Be clear:** No ambiguity about the decision
3. **Explain why:** Context helps understanding
4. **Acknowledge impact:** "I know this is disappointing..."
5. **Discuss next steps:** "Here's what happens now..."

**Example:**
> "I need to share some difficult news. We won't be moving forward with your promotion this cycle. The decision came down to [specific reasons]. I know you were hoping for a different outcome, and I understand this is disappointing. Let's talk about what this means and what the path forward looks like."

## üé≠ Different 1:1 Styles by Level

### Junior Developers

**Focus:**
- Frequent guidance and teaching
- Building confidence
- Learning fundamentals
- Short-term goals

**Approach:**
- More directive coaching
- Regular check-ins (weekly)
- Lots of questions and answers
- Celebrating small wins

**Time Split:**
- 50% tactical/immediate work
- 30% skill development
- 20% career/long-term

### Confirmed Developers

**Focus:**
- Increasing autonomy
- Expanding scope
- Developing voice
- Mid-term growth

**Approach:**
- Balanced coaching
- Less frequent (weekly or bi-weekly)
- More problem-solving together
- Recognizing contributions

**Time Split:**
- 40% tactical/immediate work
- 30% skill development
- 30% career/long-term

### Senior Developers

**Focus:**
- Leadership development
- Cross-team impact
- Mentoring others
- Strategic thinking

**Approach:**
- Partnership model
- Bi-weekly cadence
- More peer-to-peer dialogue
- Focus on influence and impact

**Time Split:**
- 20% tactical/immediate work
- 30% skill/leadership development
- 50% career/strategic

### Staff and Beyond

**Focus:**
- Organizational impact
- Strategic initiatives
- Developing others
- Long-term vision

**Approach:**
- Collaborative partnership
- Flexible cadence
- Strategic discussions
- Leveraging their expertise

**Time Split:**
- 10% tactical/immediate work
- 20% skill development
- 70% career/strategic/organizational

## üåê Remote 1:1 Best Practices

### Setup

**‚úÖ Do:**
- Use video (not just audio)
- Good lighting and audio
- Quiet space without distractions
- Reliable internet connection
- Share screen when reviewing documents

**‚ùå Don't:**
- Take calls while walking (hard to focus)
- Join from noisy locations
- Use phone if laptop available
- Multitask or check email

### Building Connection Remotely

**Strategies:**
- Start with personal check-in
- Use virtual backgrounds for fun
- Share your space/context
- Send memes or articles between meetings
- Occasional informal "coffee chats"
- Celebrate wins visibly (emoji reactions, gifs)

### Documentation is Critical

With remote work:
- Shared docs are even more important
- Use collaborative tools (Notion, Google Docs)
- Make notes visible in real-time
- Action items in project management tools
- Follow up in writing after calls
