# How to Have Effective Career Conversations

A step-by-step guide to conducting meaningful career development discussions with your ICs.

---

## üéØ Overview

Career conversations are the foundation of IC development. Done well, they provide clarity, motivation, and direction. Done poorly, they create confusion and disengagement.

**What makes a career conversation effective:**
- Two-way dialogue, not one-way lecture
- IC drives their own development
- Clear understanding of current state and path forward
- Actionable next steps
- Regular follow-up

---

## üìÖ Types of Career Conversations

### 1. Initial Career Conversation (First 1-2 weeks)

**Purpose:** Understand where they are and where they want to go

**Duration:** 45-60 minutes

**When:** Within first 2 weeks of managing them

**What to cover:**
- Their career goals and aspirations
- What motivates them
- How they learn best
- What support they need
- Their understanding of career framework

---

### 2. Quarterly Career Check-in

**Purpose:** Review progress, adjust plans, provide clarity on trajectory

**Duration:** 45-60 minutes

**When:** Every 3 months

**What to cover:**
- Progress on development goals
- Assessment of current level performance
- Gap analysis for next level
- Upcoming opportunities
- Adjustments to plan

---

### 3. Promotion Discussion

**Purpose:** Discuss readiness for promotion or timeline to get there

**Duration:** 60 minutes

**When:** When they ask, or when you assess they're close

**What to cover:**
- Current performance vs. next level expectations
- Evidence of next-level work
- Timeline and what's needed
- Realistic expectations

---

### 4. Performance Issue Conversation

**Purpose:** Address performance gaps and create improvement plan

**Duration:** 30-45 minutes

**When:** As soon as performance issues are clear

**What to cover:**
- Specific performance concerns
- Gap between expected and actual
- Consequences of not improving
- Support and timeline for improvement

---

## üó£Ô∏è Step-by-Step Guide: Quarterly Career Check-in

### Before the Meeting (Your Preparation)

**1. Review their current performance (15 minutes)**
- Look at recent work and projects
- Review 1:1 notes from past quarter
- Check their development goals
- Gather feedback from peers/stakeholders

**2. Assess against framework (15 minutes)**
- Compare their work to level expectations
- Identify strengths and growth areas
- Note evidence for each pillar
- Determine trajectory (promotion track, solid at level, concerns)

**3. Identify opportunities (10 minutes)**
- What projects are coming up?
- What stretch opportunities exist?
- Who could mentor them?
- What resources could help?

**4. Prepare your talking points (10 minutes)**
- 2-3 key strengths to reinforce
- 1-2 development areas to focus on
- Specific examples ready
- Next steps in mind

**Total prep time: ~50 minutes**

---

### Before the Meeting (IC Preparation)

**Send them prep questions 3-5 days before:**

```
Hi [Name],

We have our quarterly career check-in on [date]. To make it productive, 
please think about these questions before we meet:

1. What are you most proud of from the past quarter?
2. What has been challenging or frustrating?
3. What do you want to learn or develop next?
4. How do you feel about your progress toward [career goal]?
5. What support or opportunities would be most helpful?

Looking forward to our conversation!
```

---

### During the Meeting: The Structure

**Part 1: Their Self-Assessment (15 minutes)**

**Your opening:**
"I'd love to hear your perspective first. How do you feel the past quarter has gone?"

**Listen for:**
- Self-awareness (do they see what you see?)
- Motivation level
- Perceived obstacles
- Areas they want to develop

**Ask follow-up questions:**
- "What made you most proud?"
- "What was harder than expected?"
- "How do you feel you're progressing?"

**Don't interrupt or correct yet‚Äîjust listen and understand.**

---

**Part 2: Your Assessment (15 minutes)**

**Share your perspective:**
"Here's what I've observed over the past quarter..."

**Structure your feedback:**

**Strengths (2-3 specific examples):**
"You've really excelled at [X]. For example, [specific situation]. This demonstrates [skill at current/next level]."

**Growth Areas (1-2 specific examples):**
"An area for development is [Y]. I noticed [specific situation]. To get to [next level/improve], I'd like to see [specific behavior]."

**Overall assessment:**
"Overall, here's where I see you relative to your level..."
- Exceeding expectations at current level
- Meeting expectations solidly
- Still developing in some areas
- Concerns about performance

---

**Part 3: Gap Analysis (10 minutes)**

**If they want promotion:**
"Let's look at what [next level] requires vs. where you are today."

**Pull up the framework and walk through it together.**

**For each criterion:**
- ‚úÖ "You're demonstrating this consistently"
- üü° "You're showing signs of this, need more consistency"
- ‚ùå "This is a gap we need to focus on"

**Be specific:**
"For 'Technical Leadership' at Senior level, I need to see you leading architecture decisions. You're contributing to discussions, but not yet driving them."

---

**Part 4: Development Plan (15 minutes)**

**Collaboratively set goals:**
"Based on where you want to go and where you are, what should we focus on next quarter?"

**Ask them first:**
"What do you think would be most valuable to work on?"

**Then add your perspective:**
"I'd also suggest focusing on [X] because [reason]."

**Define 2-3 specific goals:**
- What will they do?
- How will success be measured?
- What support do they need?
- When will we check in?

**Document it:**
"I'll update your development plan with these goals and share it with you."

---

**Part 5: Wrap-up (5 minutes)**

**Summarize:**
"Let me recap what we've agreed to..."

**Next steps:**
- "I'll [action you'll take]"
- "You'll [action they'll take]"
- "We'll check in on this in [timeframe]"

**Check-in:**
"How are you feeling about this conversation? Any questions or concerns?"

**End on encouragement:**
"I'm excited about your growth and committed to supporting you."

---

### After the Meeting (Follow-up)

**Within 24 hours:**

1. **Update their development plan**
   - Document goals agreed upon
   - Note next steps and timeline
   - Save in shared location

2. **Send follow-up email**
   ```
   Hi [Name],
   
   Great conversation today. Here's what we agreed to:
   
   Goals for next quarter:
   1. [Goal 1] - [success criteria]
   2. [Goal 2] - [success criteria]
   3. [Goal 3] - [success criteria]
   
   Next steps:
   - I will: [your actions]
   - You will: [their actions]
   
   We'll check in on progress in our 1:1s and review fully next quarter.
   
   [Encouraging closing]
   ```

3. **Create opportunities**
   - Assign projects aligned with goals
   - Make introductions if needed
   - Allocate resources or training

4. **Calendar check-ins**
   - Monthly progress discussions
   - Next quarterly review

## üí¨ Example Career Conversations

### Example 1: Junior Developer, First Career Conversation

**Manager:** "I'm excited to work with you! I want to understand where you are in your career and where you want to go. Let's start with: what are your career goals?"

**IC:** "I want to become a Senior Developer eventually."

**Manager:** "That's great! Let's talk about what that path looks like. First, tell me what you think makes someone a Senior Developer?"

**IC:** "Um, I guess being really good at coding and having experience?"

**Manager:** "Those are part of it! Let me show you our career framework. [Pulls up framework]. We have 7 levels: Junior, Confirmed, Senior, Staff, Senior Staff, and Principal. You're currently at Junior level.

Senior is actually 3 levels above you‚Äîthe progression typically goes Junior ‚Üí Confirmed ‚Üí Senior. Most people spend 2-4 years at Confirmed before reaching Senior.

Let me show you what Confirmed looks like vs. Junior... [Walks through framework].

For now, let's focus on what it takes to get to Confirmed level, which is probably 12-24 months away if you develop well. Does that make sense?"

**IC:** "Yes, that's helpful to understand the path."

**Manager:** "Great. What excites you most about software engineering?"

**IC:** "I really like solving problems and learning new technologies."

**Manager:** "Perfect. Over the next quarter, let's focus on building your fundamentals. Here's what I'd like to see:
1. You writing code that needs fewer review iterations
2. You taking on slightly more complex tasks
3. You asking great questions when you're stuck

How does that sound?"

---

### Example 2: Confirmed Developer Asking About Promotion

**IC:** "I've been at Confirmed level for 2 years. When can I expect to get promoted to Senior?"

**Manager:** "I appreciate you asking directly. Let me show you where you are and what Senior level requires. [Pulls up framework].

You're doing well at Confirmed level‚Äîyour code quality is good, you deliver consistently, and you collaborate well with the team. Those are strengths.

For Senior level, here are the key differences I need to see:

**Technical scope:** Senior engineers lead complex, cross-team projects. Most of your work has been within our team. I need to see you owning projects that require coordinating with other teams.

**Technical leadership:** Senior engineers drive architecture decisions. You participate in design discussions, but I'd like to see you leading them‚Äîwriting proposals, getting feedback, making the call.

**Mentoring:** Senior engineers actively develop other engineers. You help when asked, but I'd like to see you proactively mentoring someone‚Äîowning their growth.

Right now, I'd say you're 12-18 months away from promotion if we focus intentionally on these areas.

Does this feel like a path you want to pursue?"

**IC:** "Yes, definitely. What specifically should I focus on?"

**Manager:** "Let's create a plan. Over the next 6 months:

1. **Lead a cross-team project:** I'll assign you the API redesign that requires working with Mobile and Frontend teams. Own it end-to-end.

2. **Drive a technical decision:** For that project, write the technical spec and lead the architecture discussion. I'll review with you, but you drive it.

3. **Mentor Alex:** Our junior developer Alex could use mentoring. Meet with them biweekly, review their code with coaching feedback, help them with their projects.

We'll check in monthly on progress. If I see consistent Senior-level work in these areas for 6+ months, I'll advocate for your promotion.

Sound good?"

---

### Example 3: Senior Developer Plateauing

**Manager:** "I want to talk about your career. You've been a Senior Developer for 4 years now, and you're doing good work. But I've noticed we haven't talked much about what's next for you. What are you thinking about your career direction?"

**IC:** "Honestly, I'm not sure. I'm comfortable where I am."

**Manager:** "That's totally valid. Not everyone wants to push toward Staff level, and that's perfectly fine. Let me ask: what keeps you engaged in your work?"

**IC:** "I like solving technical problems and working with the team."

**Manager:** "Great. So there are a few paths we could consider:

**Option 1: Deepen Senior excellence.** You become the go-to expert in specific technical domain, known for exceptional quality. You continue at Senior level but become increasingly valuable through deep expertise.

**Option 2: Pursue Staff level.** This is a bigger jump‚Äîit means organizational scope, technical strategy, developing leaders. It's more political, more meetings, less hands-on coding. Is that appealing?

**Option 3: Explore management.** Leading a team of engineers. Very different from IC work.

What sounds most interesting to you?"

**IC:** "I think Option 1. I don't really want to be in more meetings or manage people."

**Manager:** "Totally fair! So let's talk about what deep Senior excellence looks like for you. What technical area would you want to own and be known for?

[Continues conversation about becoming domain expert]

This is a completely valid career path. Not everyone needs to reach Staff. Being an exceptional Senior engineer who's an expert in critical areas is incredibly valuable."

## ‚ö†Ô∏è Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

### Pitfall 1: One-Way Lecture

**What it looks like:**
Manager talks at IC for 45 minutes about what they need to improve.

**Why it fails:**
IC isn't engaged, doesn't buy in, feels lectured.

**Fix:**
Start with their perspective. Ask questions. Make it dialogue. 70/30 rule: they talk 70%, you talk 30%.

---

### Pitfall 2: Vague Feedback

**What it looks like:**
"You need to be more senior."
"Improve your communication."
"Take more ownership."

**Why it fails:**
Not actionable. IC doesn't know what to change.

**Fix:**
Specific examples and specific next steps. "In the API project, I'd like you to write the technical spec and lead the architecture discussion."

---

### Pitfall 3: False Hope

**What it looks like:**
"You're doing great! Keep it up!" (when they're not on promotion track)
"Maybe soon!" (when promotion is 12+ months away)

**Why it fails:**
Creates unrealistic expectations. Leads to resentment later.

**Fix:**
Be honest and specific. "You're solid at your level, but promotion is 12-18 months away. Here's specifically what needs to change."

---

### Pitfall 4: Avoiding Difficult Truth

**What it looks like:**
Not telling someone they're not on promotion track.
Not addressing performance issues.
Being vague to avoid discomfort.

**Why it fails:**
Problems compound. IC is blindsided later. Trust erodes.

**Fix:**
Direct, kind honesty. "I need to be straight with you: you're not on track for promotion right now. Here's why and here's what needs to change."

---

### Pitfall 5: No Follow-Through

**What it looks like:**
Great conversation, create a plan, then never discuss it again.

**Why it fails:**
IC feels abandoned. Development doesn't happen.

**Fix:**
Monthly check-ins. Create the opportunities discussed. Follow up on commitments.
